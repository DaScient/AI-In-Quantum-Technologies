## Motivation

Ideal quantum theory describes closed systems; real devices are open, coupling relentlessly to their environment. Decoherence is not a nuisance to be wished away but the central engineering reality of the field, and learning to *model* it from data is the prerequisite for correcting it (Chapter 8), controlling around it (Chapter 7), and benchmarking honestly (Chapter 5).

Noise is also data. Calibration routines, randomised benchmarking, tomography, and syndrome measurements all produce partial views of how a device fails. The challenge is to compress those views into models that are simple enough to simulate yet faithful enough to support decisions.

```text
hardware interaction -> calibration / benchmark data -> noise model
        -> noisy simulation or mitigation strategy -> validation on fresh data
```

The arrow from model back to hardware is essential. A simulator with a noise channel is a hypothesis about a device, not the device itself. Real processors drift, leak outside the computational subspace, suffer crosstalk, and respond to control electronics in ways no small textbook channel captures completely.

## Theory

### Open quantum systems and Kraus operators

A closed quantum system evolves unitarily, $\rho \mapsto U\rho U^\dagger$. An open system interacts with an environment that is not fully observed. If the joint system-environment evolution is unitary and the environment is traced out, the system evolves by a **completely positive trace-preserving map**:

$$
\mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_k A_k \rho A_k^\dagger,
\qquad
\sum_k A_k^\dagger A_k = I.
$$

The matrices $A_k$ are **Kraus operators**. They are not unique, but the channel they define is. Complete positivity prevents unphysical states even when the system is entangled with an external reference; trace preservation conserves total probability.

Noise channels can be studied at several levels:

- microscopic Hamiltonian models of system-environment coupling;
- phenomenological channels fitted to calibration data;
- Pauli or stochastic approximations used for large-scale simulation;
- learned surrogate models used when closed forms are inadequate.

### Common single-qubit channels

A **depolarising channel** replaces the state by a random Pauli error with total probability $p$:

$$
\mathcal{D}_p(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + \frac{p}{3}(X\rho X + Y\rho Y + Z\rho Z).
$$

It is isotropic on the Bloch sphere and often used as a coarse benchmark model. Real devices rarely depolarise perfectly, but the channel is useful because it has one parameter and composes simply.

A **dephasing channel** suppresses coherence in a preferred basis while leaving populations unchanged:

$$
\mathcal{Z}_p(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + p Z\rho Z.
$$

In matrix form it damps off-diagonal entries. Dephasing is associated with fluctuations in qubit frequency and is characterised by a coherence time $T_2$.

An **amplitude damping channel** models energy relaxation, such as $|1\rangle$ decaying to $|0\rangle$. One Kraus representation is

$$
A_0 = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & \sqrt{1-\gamma}\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
A_1 = \begin{pmatrix}0 & \sqrt{\gamma} \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}.
$$

For a gate or idle time $t$, a common phenomenological relation is $\gamma = 1-e^{-t/T_1}$. Amplitude damping is not a Pauli channel because it is non-unital: it moves the maximally mixed state toward the ground state.

### $T_1$, $T_2$, and device calibration

**$T_1$** is the energy relaxation time. It measures how quickly excited-state population decays. **$T_2$** is the dephasing time. It measures how quickly phase coherence disappears. In many platforms, pure dephasing and relaxation combine approximately as

$$
\frac{1}{T_2} = \frac{1}{2T_1} + \frac{1}{T_\phi},
$$

where $T_\phi$ is the pure-dephasing time. These parameters are calibration summaries, not universal constants. They vary by qubit, day, temperature, control pulse, and neighbouring activity. A model trained on a stale calibration can be confidently wrong.

### Pauli channels and twirling

A noise process is a CPTP map. The most tractable and widely used family is the **Pauli channel**, which applies Pauli errors at random:

$$
\mathcal{E}(\rho) = p_I\,\rho + p_X X\rho X + p_Y Y\rho Y + p_Z Z\rho Z,
$$

with non-negative probabilities summing to one. Special cases include the bit-flip, phase-flip, and depolarising channels. Pauli channels are important not only because they are simple but because randomised compiling can *tailor* generic noise into Pauli form, making this model practically realisable.

**Pauli twirling** averages a channel over random Pauli conjugations. The twirled channel keeps the diagonal action in the Pauli basis and removes coherent off-diagonal terms. This is a modelling trade-off: it sacrifices detail about coherent errors to obtain a stochastic channel that is easier to compose, learn, and simulate in error-correction studies.

The diagonal of the Pauli transfer matrix — the **Pauli fidelities** $f_P = \operatorname{Tr}[P\,\mathcal{E}(P)]/2^n$ — is measurable by randomised benchmarking. For a single qubit, the error probabilities are recovered from the fidelities by the Walsh–Hadamard relation,

$$
p_I = \tfrac14(1 + f_X + f_Y + f_Z), \quad
p_X = \tfrac14(1 + f_X - f_Y - f_Z),
$$

and analogously for $p_Y, p_Z$. The single scalar most often quoted, the **average gate infidelity**, follows as

$$
1 - F_\mathrm{avg} = \frac{d}{d+1}\sum_{P\neq I} p_P,
$$

with $d = 2$ for a single qubit.

### Beyond compact textbook channels

Correlated and non-Markovian noise — crosstalk, drift, leakage, pulse distortions, and $1/f$ fluctuations — is where learned, data-driven models earn their keep, since no compact closed form suffices. A useful noise model should state its scope: which qubits, which gates, which calibration window, which measurement procedure, and which validation data. Without that scope, an error probability is a number without a contract.

## From theory to code

The `aiquantum.simulate` module learns a single-qubit Pauli channel from measured fidelities, backing `/api/v1/simulate/noise_model`:

```python
from aiquantum.simulate import learn_pauli_channel, average_gate_infidelity

probs = learn_pauli_channel({"X": 0.98, "Y": 0.98, "Z": 0.99})
print(probs)                              # {'I': ..., 'X': ..., 'Y': ..., 'Z': ...}
print(average_gate_infidelity(probs))     # a single quality number
```

Clipping and renormalisation guarantee a valid probability vector even when measured fidelities are noisy.

### Simulated noise with Qiskit Aer

This example constructs a simulator noise model. It does not claim to reproduce any particular real backend unless its parameters are fitted and validated against that backend's data.

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator
from qiskit_aer.noise import NoiseModel, depolarizing_error, thermal_relaxation_error

noise_model = NoiseModel()
noise_model.add_all_qubit_quantum_error(depolarizing_error(0.001, 1), ["x", "sx"])
noise_model.add_all_qubit_quantum_error(depolarizing_error(0.01, 2), ["cx"])

# Thermal relaxation during a 100 ns single-qubit gate.
t1 = 50e-6
t2 = 70e-6  # Physically plausible here because T2 <= 2*T1.
gate_time = 100e-9
thermal_error = thermal_relaxation_error(t1, t2, gate_time)
noise_model.add_all_qubit_quantum_error(thermal_error, ["h"])

qc = QuantumCircuit(2, 2)
qc.h(0)
qc.cx(0, 1)
qc.measure([0, 1], [0, 1])

backend = AerSimulator(noise_model=noise_model)
compiled = transpile(qc, backend)
counts = backend.run(compiled, shots=2000).result().get_counts()
print(counts)
```

The counts are noisy simulated samples. Real-hardware counts would include the actual backend name, queue time, calibration snapshot, transpiled layout, and measurement mitigation choices.

### Cirq noise channels

Cirq exposes channels as operations that can be inserted directly into a circuit. The density-matrix simulator propagates the noisy state classically.

```python
import cirq

q0, q1 = cirq.LineQubit.range(2)
circuit = cirq.Circuit(
    cirq.H(q0),
    cirq.CNOT(q0, q1),
    cirq.depolarize(p=0.005).on(q0),
    cirq.amplitude_damp(gamma=0.02).on(q1),
    cirq.measure(q0, q1, key="m"),
)

simulator = cirq.DensityMatrixSimulator()
result = simulator.run(circuit, repetitions=1000)
print(result.histogram(key="m"))
```

Again, this is a controlled simulated experiment. It is valuable for understanding mechanisms and testing pipelines, but real-hardware noise requires empirical calibration and validation.

## Summary

Noise is the defining constraint of present-day quantum hardware. Open-system evolution is described by CPTP maps and Kraus operators. Depolarising, dephasing, and amplitude-damping channels capture important limiting behaviours, while $T_1$ and $T_2$ connect models to calibration practice. The Pauli channel gives a tractable, learnable approximation; its fidelities and error probabilities are two faces of the same object, linked by a Hadamard transform. Simulated noise is a useful hypothesis, not a substitute for real-hardware evidence.
