## Motivation

Ideal quantum theory describes closed systems; real devices are open, coupling
relentlessly to their environment. Decoherence is not a nuisance to be wished
away but the central engineering reality of the field, and learning to *model* it
from data is the prerequisite for correcting it (Chapter 8), controlling around
it (Chapter 7), and benchmarking honestly (Chapter 5).

## Theory

A noise process is a CPTP map. The most tractable and widely used family is the
**Pauli channel**, which applies Pauli errors at random:

$$
\mathcal{E}(\rho) = p_I\,\rho + p_X X\rho X + p_Y Y\rho Y + p_Z Z\rho Z,
$$

with non-negative probabilities summing to one. Special cases include the
bit-flip, phase-flip, and depolarising channels. Pauli channels are important not
only because they are simple but because randomised compiling can *tailor* generic
noise into Pauli form, making this model practically realisable.

The diagonal of the Pauli transfer matrix — the **Pauli fidelities** $f_P =
\operatorname{Tr}[P\,\mathcal{E}(P)]/2^n$ — is measurable by randomised
benchmarking. For a single qubit, the error probabilities are recovered from the
fidelities by the Walsh–Hadamard relation,

$$
p_I = \tfrac14(1 + f_X + f_Y + f_Z), \quad
p_X = \tfrac14(1 + f_X - f_Y - f_Z),
$$

and analogously for $p_Y, p_Z$. The single scalar most often quoted, the
**average gate infidelity**, follows as $1 - F_\mathrm{avg} = \frac{d}{d+1}
\sum_{P\neq I} p_P$ with $d = 2$.

Beyond Pauli channels lie amplitude damping (energy relaxation, $T_1$) and phase
damping (dephasing, $T_2$), which are not Pauli but can be approximated as such
for many purposes. Correlated and non-Markovian noise — crosstalk, drift, $1/f$
fluctuations — is where learned, data-driven models earn their keep, since no
compact closed form suffices.

## From theory to code

The `aiquantum.simulate` module learns a single-qubit Pauli channel from measured
fidelities, backing `/api/v1/simulate/noise_model`:

```python
from aiquantum.simulate import learn_pauli_channel, average_gate_infidelity

probs = learn_pauli_channel({"X": 0.98, "Y": 0.98, "Z": 0.99})
print(probs)                              # {'I': ..., 'X': ..., 'Y': ..., 'Z': ...}
print(average_gate_infidelity(probs))     # a single quality number
```

Clipping and renormalisation guarantee a valid probability vector even when
measured fidelities are noisy.

## Summary

Noise is the defining constraint of present-day quantum hardware. The Pauli
channel gives a tractable, learnable model; its fidelities and error
probabilities are two faces of the same object, linked by a Hadamard transform.
