## Learning objectives

- Distinguish pure-state vectors from density-matrix descriptions and know when each is appropriate.
- Reconstruct a density matrix from informationally complete measurement data by linear inversion.
- Express measurement records as Pauli expectation vectors with finite-shot uncertainty.
- Represent quantum *processes* through the Choi matrix, chi matrix, and Pauli transfer matrix.
- Appreciate the exponential cost of full tomography, why physicality projection is needed, and where learned estimators enter.

## Motivation

Before we can learn from quantum data, we must agree on how a quantum object becomes data. A state or process is never handed to us as a matrix; it is inferred from the statistics of measurements. **Tomography** is that inference, and it is the cleanest illustration of the central tension of the field: complete characterisation costs resources exponential in system size, so approximation — often learned — is mandatory.

The representational choice matters. A variational algorithm may only need a few observables; a calibration campaign may need a process matrix; an error-correction decoder may need a compact noise channel. Treating all of these as “the state of the device” hides the distinction between the physical object, the measurement procedure, and the classical data structure used for learning. In practice, the workflow is:

```text
prepare quantum system -> choose measurement settings -> collect shots
        -> estimate expectation values -> reconstruct or learn representation
        -> validate physical constraints -> deploy in analysis or control
```

Classical simulation and real-hardware execution must be separated. A simulator can return an exact state vector or density matrix because the classical program stores the amplitudes. A hardware device returns samples: bit strings, timestamps, discriminator outputs, calibration metadata. Tomography is the bridge from those samples to a compact mathematical representation.

## Theory

### State vectors and density matrices

A closed, perfectly isolated system can be represented by a **state vector** $|\psi\rangle$ in a Hilbert space. For $n$ qubits,

$$
|\psi\rangle = \sum_{x \in \{0,1\}^n} \alpha_x |x\rangle, \qquad \sum_x |\alpha_x|^2 = 1.
$$

This vector is economical for pure states but too restrictive for realistic experiments. Most laboratory states are **mixed states**: ensembles arising from preparation uncertainty, coupling to an environment, or deliberately classical randomisation. They are represented by a **density matrix**

$$
\rho = \sum_k p_k |\psi_k\rangle\langle\psi_k|,
$$

where $p_k \ge 0$ and $\sum_k p_k = 1$. A valid density matrix is Hermitian, positive semidefinite, and trace one. Pure states satisfy $\rho^2 = \rho$ and $\operatorname{Tr}(\rho^2)=1$; mixed states have $\operatorname{Tr}(\rho^2)<1$.

The density-matrix representation is not merely a mathematical convenience. It is the representation that makes measurement probabilities linear:

$$
p(m) = \operatorname{Tr}(M_m \rho),
$$

for measurement operators $M_m$. Linear dependence is the reason tomography can be expressed as a system of linear equations.

### Informationally complete measurements

A measurement design is **informationally complete** when its outcome probabilities determine $\rho$ uniquely. For qubits, tensor-product Pauli operators

$$
\mathcal{P}_n = \{I, X, Y, Z\}^{\otimes n}
$$

form an orthogonal operator basis under the Hilbert–Schmidt inner product:

$$
\operatorname{Tr}(P_i P_j) = 2^n \delta_{ij}.
$$

Consequently any $n$-qubit state can be expanded as

$$
\rho = \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_{P \in \mathcal{P}_n} r_P P,
\qquad r_P = \operatorname{Tr}(P\rho) = \langle P\rangle.
$$

The coefficients $r_P$ are a **Pauli expectation vector**. The identity component is always $r_I=1$; the remaining $4^n-1$ real numbers are the state parameters. This is the algebraic origin of the exponential wall. Full tomography of ten qubits already asks for over one million expectation values before considering repeated shots.

Expectation values are estimated from samples. If a Pauli observable has outcomes $\pm1$ and is measured $N$ times, the estimator

$$
\hat r_P = \frac{n_+ - n_-}{N}
$$

has variance $(1-r_P^2)/N$. The $1/\sqrt{N}$ sampling law is benign for one observable but expensive when multiplied across exponentially many settings.

### Linear-inversion state tomography

**Linear inversion** substitutes estimated expectations into the Pauli expansion:

$$
\hat\rho_{\mathrm{raw}} = \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_P \hat r_P P.
$$

With infinite shots and an informationally complete design, this recovers the true state. With finite shots, the raw matrix is Hermitian and trace one but may have negative eigenvalues. Negative eigenvalues are not exotic physics; they are a statistical artefact of solving an unconstrained linear problem.

A common repair is **physicality projection**: find a nearby positive semidefinite, trace-one matrix. The simplest version diagonalises $\hat\rho_{\mathrm{raw}}$, clips or projects eigenvalues onto the probability simplex, and reconstructs the matrix. More sophisticated maximum-likelihood and Bayesian methods impose physicality during estimation, but linear inversion remains the transparent baseline.

The engineering analogy is sensor calibration. Linear inversion is the unregularised least-squares calibration; projection is the step that enforces conservation laws and admissible parameter ranges.

### Processes: CP maps, Choi matrices, chi matrices, and PTMs

A quantum operation is a **completely positive trace-preserving map** (CPTP map) $\mathcal{E}$. Complete positivity ensures that $\mathcal{E}\otimes I$ maps every joint state to a valid joint state even when the system is entangled with an ancilla. Trace preservation ensures probabilities sum to one.

One representation is the **Kraus form**

$$
\mathcal{E}(\rho) = \sum_k A_k \rho A_k^\dagger, \qquad \sum_k A_k^\dagger A_k = I.
$$

A second is the **Choi matrix**, obtained by applying the channel to half of a maximally entangled state:

$$
J(\mathcal{E}) = (\mathcal{E}\otimes I)(|\Phi\rangle\langle\Phi|),
\qquad |\Phi\rangle = \sum_i |i\rangle|i\rangle.
$$

The map is completely positive exactly when $J(\mathcal{E}) \succeq 0$; it is trace preserving when the appropriate partial trace equals the identity. These constraints make the Choi picture natural for physicality checks.

The **chi matrix** expands the map in an operator basis, often Paulis:

$$
\mathcal{E}(\rho)=\sum_{m,n}\chi_{mn} E_m \rho E_n^\dagger.
$$

The chi matrix is useful when one wants to report coherent and incoherent error components relative to a chosen gate basis. The **Pauli transfer matrix** (PTM),

$$
R_{ij} = \frac{1}{2^n}\operatorname{Tr}\!\left[P_i\,\mathcal{E}(P_j)\right],
$$

is a real matrix whose columns describe how each input Pauli operator is transformed. Trace preservation fixes the identity row; complete positivity constrains the spectrum through the equivalent Choi matrix. The PTM composes by ordinary matrix multiplication, making it ideal for reasoning about gate sequences and noise.

Process tomography prepares a spanning set of input states, applies the unknown process, performs state tomography on the outputs, and solves for the map. As with state tomography, finite data can produce a non-CPTP raw estimate, so projection or constrained estimation is required before the object is used in simulation, benchmarking, or control.

## From theory to code

```python
from aiquantum.quantum import linear_inversion_tomography, linear_inversion_process_tomography

# Reconstruct |+> from <X>=1.
rho = linear_inversion_tomography({"X": 1.0}, n_qubits=1)

# A process that maps X -> X (identity on the X axis).
ptm = linear_inversion_process_tomography({"X": {"X": 1.0}}, n_qubits=1)
print(ptm.shape)   # (4, 4)
```

These functions back the `/api/v1/quantum/state_tomography` and `/process_tomography` endpoints. They are the canonical offline reference for this chapter: simple input dictionaries of Pauli expectations become matrices suitable for analysis.

### Estimating Pauli expectations with Qiskit

The following static example illustrates how hardware-style counts become a Pauli expectation. On real hardware the counts would be noisy samples from a backend; on a simulator they are sampled from an ideal model unless a noise model is supplied.

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator

shots = 4000
qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1)
qc.h(0)                  # prepare |+>
# The second Hadamard rotates the X eigenbasis to computational Z before measurement.
qc.h(0)
qc.measure(0, 0)

backend = AerSimulator()
compiled = transpile(qc, backend)
counts = backend.run(compiled, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

n0 = counts.get("0", 0)  # X = +1 outcome
n1 = counts.get("1", 0)  # X = -1 outcome
x_expectation = (n0 - n1) / shots
print(x_expectation)
```

For exact simulator-side algebra rather than shot records, `qiskit.quantum_info` exposes density matrices and Pauli operators directly:

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import DensityMatrix, Pauli, Statevector

qc = QuantumCircuit(1)
qc.h(0)
rho = DensityMatrix(Statevector.from_instruction(qc))
expect_x = rho.expectation_value(Pauli("X")).real
print(expect_x)  # 1.0 for an ideal simulator
```

### Density-matrix simulation with Cirq

Cirq can propagate density matrices explicitly, which is useful when the circuit includes noisy channels. This is still classical simulation, not hardware execution.

```python
import cirq

q = cirq.LineQubit(0)
circuit = cirq.Circuit(
    cirq.H(q),
    cirq.depolarize(p=0.02).on(q),
)

simulator = cirq.DensityMatrixSimulator()
result = simulator.simulate(circuit)
print(result.final_density_matrix)
```

The density matrix printed by the simulator is the complete simulated state for one qubit. A real device would provide samples from measurements, and the density matrix would have to be inferred.

## Summary

Tomography turns measurements into matrices and exposes the exponential wall that motivates machine learning. State vectors describe ideal pure states; density matrices describe mixed states and make measurements linear. Pauli expectation vectors provide an informationally complete coordinate system for qubits. Linear inversion plus physicality projection is the honest baseline; process tomography extends the same logic to CPTP maps represented as Choi matrices, chi matrices, or Pauli transfer matrices.
