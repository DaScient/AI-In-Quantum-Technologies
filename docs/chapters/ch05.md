## Learning objectives

- Characterise the kinds of data quantum experiments produce and the schemas they follow.
- Distinguish raw measurement records, shot tables, calibration logs, and derived feature tables.
- Quantify sampling noise and recognise how finite shots affect labels and targets.
- Identify train/test leakage modes that are specific to quantum hardware and simulation.
- Use the repository's dataset layer to obtain reproducible, schema-faithful data.

## Motivation

A learning system is only as good as its data, and quantum data are peculiar: they are expensive to collect, noisy by construction, and often proprietary to a single device or laboratory. Treating data curation as a first-class scientific activity — rather than an afterthought — is what separates reproducible quantum machine learning from anecdote.

Quantum experiments produce data at several scales. A single shot may be a bit string such as `0101`. A circuit execution produces a histogram of many such shots. A calibration cycle records qubit frequencies, readout errors, gate durations, and relaxation times. A benchmark archive may join circuit metadata, backend properties, transpiler settings, and outcomes over weeks. Machine-learning models can consume any of these layers, but only if the schema preserves provenance.

```text
experiment design -> circuit / pulse schedule -> backend + calibration epoch
        -> shots and counts -> derived observables -> model-ready table
        -> train/validation/test split with leakage checks
```

The central discipline is to keep raw observations, derived estimates, and labels separate. A Pauli expectation, a syndrome vector, and an energy estimate may all be computed from the same shots, but they are not independent examples.

## Theory

### What counts as quantum data?

**Quantum data** are classical records whose statistical structure is generated by quantum preparation, evolution, and measurement. The records are classical because measurement outcomes are classical; the source model is quantum. Common families include:

- **Measurement records**: per-shot bit strings, analog readout voltages, timestamps, heralding flags, and measurement bases.
- **Shot tables**: grouped counts by circuit, parameter setting, observable, and backend. These are the raw material of tomography, variational algorithms, and benchmarking.
- **Calibration logs**: time-indexed device properties such as $T_1$, $T_2$, readout assignment matrices, gate error estimates, qubit frequencies, and coupling maps.
- **Syndrome corpora**: stabiliser measurement outcomes paired with injected or inferred errors for decoder training.
- **Property databases**: molecular or materials structures paired with computed or measured targets, such as energies, gaps, or critical temperatures.
- **Simulation archives**: exact state vectors, density matrices, tensor-network contractions, or noiseless labels generated by classical algorithms.

The last category must be labelled clearly. Classical simulation is indispensable for controlled experiments and debugging, but it is not evidence that a model will perform on hardware affected by drift, crosstalk, leakage, and readout errors.

### Sampling and shot-noise statistics

Quantum measurement is probabilistic. If an outcome $x$ has probability $p_x$, then $N$ shots produce a count $C_x$ distributed as a binomial or multinomial random variable. For a two-outcome observable with values $\pm1$,

$$
\hat\mu = \frac{n_+ - n_-}{N}, \qquad
\operatorname{Var}(\hat\mu)=\frac{1-\mu^2}{N}.
$$

This variance appears as label noise whenever expectations are used as supervised targets. Increasing shots reduces statistical uncertainty as $1/\sqrt{N}$, not exponentially. Systematic errors, however, do not average away: a biased readout discriminator, a stale calibration, or a mis-specified noise model can persist across arbitrarily many shots.

Good dataset schemas therefore store at least:

- circuit identifier and parameters;
- measurement basis and observable definition;
- shot count and raw counts or per-shot records;
- backend or simulator identity;
- calibration timestamp and relevant device properties;
- random seeds and transpilation settings for simulated data;
- licence and redistribution constraints.

### Train/test leakage in quantum settings

**Train/test leakage** occurs when information from the evaluation set influences training. Quantum workflows introduce leakage modes that are easy to miss:

- **Circuit-family leakage**: random parameter instances from the same ansatz appear in both train and test, so the model memorises a smooth local family rather than learning a transferable representation.
- **Calibration-epoch leakage**: data from the same hardware calibration cycle are split randomly, allowing the model to exploit device-specific drift signatures.
- **Shot reuse leakage**: the same underlying shots are aggregated into multiple derived observables placed in different splits.
- **Simulator-label leakage**: noiseless simulated labels are mixed with noisy hardware features without marking their source.
- **Transpilation leakage**: compiled layouts or basis-gate choices encode backend identity in a way that makes a benchmark easier than intended.

Robust splits are often grouped by circuit family, molecule, lattice size, device, or time window. The correct grouping depends on the scientific claim. If the claim is “generalises to tomorrow's calibration,” split by time. If it is “generalises to unseen circuits,” split by circuit family.

### Data formats and schemas

Quantum data move through a mixture of formats. Small shot tables are naturally represented as CSV or JSON Lines. Large arrays of bit strings and observables fit HDF5, Zarr, Parquet, or Arrow-style columnar formats. Metadata should be machine-readable, not hidden in filenames.

A minimal shot-table schema might contain:

| column | meaning |
| --- | --- |
| `experiment_id` | logical experiment or benchmark run |
| `circuit_id` | stable hash or name of the circuit |
| `backend` | simulator or hardware backend |
| `calibration_id` | calibration epoch or snapshot |
| `basis` | measurement basis such as `Z`, `XX`, or `XYZ` |
| `bitstring` | observed classical outcome |
| `shots` | number of repeated preparations represented by the row |
| `count` | count for the bit string when rows are aggregated |

The principle is simple: store enough information that a reader can recompute every derived label and decide whether two examples are independent.

## From theory to code

The repository's `datasets` package combines a registry of external benchmarks with synthetic generators:

```python
from datasets import list_datasets, describe, synthetic_syndrome_dataset

print(describe("stim_syndromes").source_url)
batch = synthetic_syndrome_dataset(n_samples=1000, n_data=3, error_rate=0.1)
print(batch["errors"].shape, batch["syndromes"].shape)   # (1000, 3) (1000, 2)
```

The synthetic syndrome generator enforces the exact parity relation $s_i = e_i \oplus e_{i+1}$, so a decoder trained on it learns the true structure of the code.

### Qiskit counts to a shot table

Qiskit primitives and Aer-style simulators return counts that can be normalised into a table. The example below uses a simulator; replacing it with a hardware backend would require authentication, queueing, and careful calibration metadata.

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile
from qiskit_aer import AerSimulator

qc = QuantumCircuit(2, 2)
qc.h(0)
qc.cx(0, 1)
qc.measure([0, 1], [0, 1])

shots = 1024
backend = AerSimulator()
compiled = transpile(qc, backend)
counts = backend.run(compiled, shots=shots).result().get_counts()

shot_table = [
    {
        "experiment_id": "bell-demo",
        "circuit_id": "bell-h-cx",
        "backend": "AerSimulator",
        "basis": "ZZ",
        "bitstring": bitstring,
        "shots": shots,
        "count": count,
    }
    for bitstring, count in counts.items()
]
print(shot_table)
```

A primitive-style sampling sketch follows the same logic: the sampler produces quasi-distributions or sampled results, which should be saved with circuit and backend metadata rather than as anonymous arrays.

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.primitives import StatevectorSampler

qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1)
qc.h(0)
qc.measure(0, 0)

sampler = StatevectorSampler(default_shots=256)
job = sampler.run([qc])
result = job.result()[0]
counts = result.data.c.get_counts()
print(counts)
```

### PennyLane sampling sketch

PennyLane is convenient when datasets arise from parameterised circuits. The code below samples from a small circuit on a local simulator. It is a simulated dataset, not a hardware record.

```python
import pennylane as qml
import numpy as np

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=2, shots=500)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def circuit(theta):
    qml.RY(theta, wires=0)
    qml.CNOT(wires=[0, 1])
    return qml.sample(wires=[0, 1])

angles = np.linspace(0.0, np.pi, 5)
dataset = [
    {
        "theta": float(theta),
        "samples": circuit(theta).tolist(),
        "source": "simulator",  # Hardware rows would also require device and calibration fields.
    }
    for theta in angles
]
```

The same schema could be used for hardware execution, but the source, device name, compilation settings, and calibration timestamp would become mandatory fields.

## Summary

Data are the substrate of quantum AI. Measurement records, shot tables, calibration logs, syndrome corpora, and property databases each encode different parts of an experiment. Provenance, bias, leakage, and licensing are scientific concerns, not clerical ones. Schema-faithful generators make reproducibility the default, while clear source labels prevent simulated data from being mistaken for hardware evidence.
