> Part II: Quantum Data Across Scales

## Learning objectives

  follow.

## Motivation

A learning system is only as good as its data, and quantum data are peculiar:
they are expensive to collect, noisy by construction, and often proprietary to a
single device or laboratory. Treating data curation as a first-class scientific
activity — rather than an afterthought — is what separates reproducible quantum
machine learning from anecdote.

## Theory

Quantum datasets fall into a few broad families, each with a characteristic
schema. **Measurement records** are bit-strings sampled from a circuit, the raw
material of tomography and randomness certification. **Calibration data** pair
control parameters with observed fidelities and underpin noise-model learning
(Chapter 6) and control (Chapter 7). **Syndrome corpora** pair injected errors
with their measured stabiliser outcomes and train decoders (Chapter 8).
**Property databases** — QM9, the Materials Project, SuperCon — pair a structure
with a computed or measured property and train regressors and generative models
(Chapters 10–11).

Three concerns deserve constant attention. *Provenance*: which device, which
calibration epoch, which simulator settings produced the data? Drift means a
model trained on yesterday's calibration may fail today. *Bias*: a decoder
trained only on low error rates generalises poorly to the regime where it matters
most. *Licensing*: experimental datasets carry heterogeneous terms, and
redistribution without care is both a legal and an ethical hazard (Chapter 19).

Because faithful raw archives are large and licence-encumbered, reproducibility
is best served by *generators* that emit data in the exact schema of a benchmark.
A generator captures the statistical structure — finite-shot noise on Pauli
expectations, parity relations between errors and syndromes — without shipping
gigabytes, and it lets every reader run every example offline.

## From theory to code

The repository's `datasets` package combines a registry of external benchmarks
with synthetic generators:

```python
from datasets import list_datasets, describe, synthetic_syndrome_dataset

print(describe("stim_syndromes").source_url)
batch = synthetic_syndrome_dataset(n_samples=1000, n_data=3, error_rate=0.1)
print(batch["errors"].shape, batch["syndromes"].shape)   # (1000, 3) (1000, 2)
```

The synthetic syndrome generator enforces the exact parity relation
$s_i = e_i \oplus e_{i+1}$, so a decoder trained on it learns the true structure
of the code.

## Summary

Data are the substrate of quantum AI. Provenance, bias, and licensing are
scientific concerns, not clerical ones, and schema-faithful generators make
reproducibility the default rather than the exception.
