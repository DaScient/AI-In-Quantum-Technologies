## Motivation

The twentieth century inherited quantum mechanics as a theory of matter — of
electrons, photons, and atoms. The twenty-first century has increasingly come to
read it as a theory of *information*: what can be known, copied, transmitted, and
computed when the carriers of information obey the superposition principle. This
reframing, catalysed by the work of Wiesner, Bennett, Deutsch, Shor, and many
others, is the intellectual foundation of every chapter that follows. Artificial
intelligence enters precisely because the information content of a quantum system
grows exponentially with its size, outstripping our capacity to reason about it
unaided.

## Theory

A pure state of an isolated quantum system is a unit vector $|\psi\rangle$ in a
complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$. For a single qubit, $\mathcal{H} =
\mathbb{C}^2$, and any state can be written

$$
|\psi\rangle = \cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|0\rangle + e^{i\varphi}\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|1\rangle,
$$

a point on the Bloch sphere parameterised by $(\theta, \varphi)$. Composite
systems live in tensor products, $\mathcal{H}_{AB} = \mathcal{H}_A \otimes
\mathcal{H}_B$, and it is this tensor structure — not any single particle — that
makes quantum information rich and counter-intuitive. Entangled states such as
the Bell state $|\Phi^+\rangle = (|00\rangle + |11\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$ cannot be
factorised and exhibit correlations with no classical analogue.

When a system is not perfectly known, or is entangled with an inaccessible
environment, its state is described by a **density matrix** $\rho$ — a positive
semi-definite, unit-trace, Hermitian operator. The density matrix is the central
object of this book because real devices are always open and noisy. Its
informational content is summarised by quantities such as the purity
$\operatorname{Tr}(\rho^2)$ and the von Neumann entropy $S(\rho) =
-\operatorname{Tr}(\rho \log \rho)$, which measures, in bits, our ignorance of
the system.

Measurement is the bridge between the quantum and classical worlds. The Born
rule assigns to an observable — a Hermitian operator $O$ — the expectation value
$\langle O \rangle = \operatorname{Tr}(O\rho)$. Because a single measurement
yields only one eigenvalue, characterising a state requires many repetitions
across an informationally complete set of observables, a theme that recurs in
tomography (Chapter 4) and metrology (Chapter 9).

## From theory to code

The [`aiquantum.linalg`](../api.md) module provides the primitives introduced
here: the Pauli basis, fidelity, purity, and von Neumann entropy.

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.linalg import purity, von_neumann_entropy

rho_pure = np.array([[1, 0], [0, 0]], dtype=complex)
print(purity(rho_pure))            # 1.0  -> a pure state
print(von_neumann_entropy(rho_pure))  # 0.0  -> no uncertainty

rho_mixed = np.eye(2) / 2
print(purity(rho_mixed))           # 0.5  -> maximally mixed
print(von_neumann_entropy(rho_mixed))  # 1.0 bit
```

## Summary

Quantum mechanics, read as information science, hands us a precise vocabulary —
states, operators, measurements, entropy — for the rest of the book. The density
matrix is our universal description of real quantum hardware, and its exponential
dimensionality is exactly the pressure point at which machine learning becomes
indispensable.
