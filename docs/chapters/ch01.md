## Motivation

The twentieth century inherited quantum mechanics as a theory of matter — of
electrons, photons, and atoms. The twenty-first century has increasingly come to
read it as a theory of *information*: what can be known, copied, transmitted, and
computed when the carriers of information obey the superposition principle. This
reframing, catalysed by the work of Wiesner, Bennett, Deutsch, Shor, and many
others, is the intellectual foundation of every chapter that follows. Artificial
intelligence enters precisely because the information content of a quantum system
grows exponentially with its size, outstripping our capacity to reason about it
unaided.

The information-science view is not a metaphor. It is an engineering discipline.
A photonic polarisation, a superconducting circuit mode, a trapped-ion hyperfine
level, and an electron spin are physically different, yet each can instantiate the
same abstract qubit. The abstraction permits common compilers, error models,
learning algorithms, and verification tools. It also forces intellectual honesty:
a simulator that stores a full state vector on a classical computer is not the
same object as a noisy quantum processor. The former gives exact amplitudes up to
floating-point error; the latter returns samples affected by calibration drift,
shot noise, decoherence, crosstalk, and imperfect measurement. This distinction
will matter whenever AI systems are trained on simulated data and deployed on
hardware.

## Theory

### Hilbert space as the state space of information

A pure state of an isolated quantum system is a unit vector $|\psi\rangle$ in a
complex **Hilbert space** $\mathcal{H}$. The global phase of a state is not
observable: $|\psi\rangle$ and $e^{i\alpha}|\psi\rangle$ encode the same physical
state. What matters are inner products, amplitudes, and relative phases. The
normalisation condition

$$
\langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1
$$

turns amplitudes into probabilities after measurement. This is the first crucial
contrast with classical probability. A classical bit is in one of two states, or
our ignorance is represented by a probability distribution over those states. A
qubit has complex amplitudes whose phases can interfere before probabilities are
formed. Interference is the mechanism behind amplitude amplification, phase
estimation, variational circuits, and quantum sensing.

An observable is a Hermitian operator $O = O^\dagger$. Its possible exact
measurement outcomes are eigenvalues, and the spectral theorem writes it as

$$
O = \sum_k \lambda_k |k\rangle\langle k|.
$$

Quantum theory therefore packages information into three related objects:

- **states**, vectors or density operators describing preparation;
- **operations**, unitary or noisy maps describing evolution;
- **measurements**, positive operators describing the extraction of classical
  records.

### Qubits and the Bloch sphere

For a single qubit, $\mathcal{H}=\mathbb{C}^2$, and any pure state can be written

$$
|\psi\rangle = \cos\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|0\rangle + e^{i\varphi}\sin\tfrac{\theta}{2}\,|1\rangle,
$$

with $0\leq\theta\leq\pi$ and $0\leq\varphi<2\pi$. This is the **Bloch sphere**:
$|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ are poles, while equal superpositions lie on the
equator. The sphere is a faithful picture only for a single qubit; two or more
qubits cannot be visualised as independent spheres without losing entanglement.
Still, it is an essential engineering diagram. Microwave pulses rotate
superconducting qubits on the sphere; trapped-ion laser pulses implement the same
mathematics with different hardware.

The Pauli matrices form the coordinate axes of the Bloch representation:

$$
X=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},\quad
Y=\begin{pmatrix}0&-i\\i&0\end{pmatrix},\quad
Z=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

A single-qubit density matrix can be written as

$$
\rho = \frac{1}{2}(I + r_x X + r_y Y + r_z Z),
$$

where $\vec r$ is the Bloch vector. Pure states have $\lVert\vec r\rVert=1$;
noise shrinks the vector into the sphere.

### Tensor products and entanglement

Composite systems live in tensor products,
$\mathcal{H}_{AB}=\mathcal{H}_A\otimes\mathcal{H}_B$. Two qubits require four
complex amplitudes, $n$ qubits require $2^n$, and this exponential scaling is the
computational pressure that motivates both quantum computing and AI-assisted
modelling. A product state factorises as

$$
|\psi\rangle_{AB}=|a\rangle_A\otimes|b\rangle_B.
$$

An **entangled** state cannot be written this way. The Bell state

$$
|\Phi^+\rangle=\frac{|00\rangle+|11\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}
$$

is the canonical example. It has perfect correlations in the computational basis,
but neither qubit individually has a definite pure state. Its reduced density
matrix is obtained by the partial trace,

$$
\rho_A = \operatorname{Tr}_B(|\Phi^+\rangle\langle\Phi^+|)=\frac{I}{2}.
$$

Entanglement is therefore not “very strong correlation” in an ordinary sense. It
is correlation that is encoded in the tensor-product structure itself. Many later
algorithms exploit it; many classical simulations struggle because of it.

A minimal circuit blueprint for creating $|\Phi^+\rangle$ is:

```text
q0: |0> ──H──■──   ->  (|00> + |11>) / sqrt(2)
             │
q1: |0> ─────X──
```

### Density matrices, mixed states, and open systems

When a system is not perfectly known, or is entangled with an inaccessible
environment, its state is described by a **density matrix** $\rho$ — a positive
semi-definite, unit-trace, Hermitian operator. The density matrix is the central
object of this book because real devices are always open and noisy. A preparation
that produces $|\psi_j\rangle$ with probability $p_j$ is represented as

$$
\rho = \sum_j p_j |\psi_j\rangle\langle\psi_j|.
$$

This formula includes classical uncertainty, but density matrices also describe
subsystems of larger entangled states. That dual role is why density matrices are
more fundamental for engineering than state vectors. A state vector is a useful
idealisation; a density matrix is the object reconstructed in tomography,
propagated by noise models, and optimised in many learning tasks.

The **purity**

$$
\gamma(\rho)=\operatorname{Tr}(\rho^2)
$$

is $1$ for pure states and $1/d$ for the maximally mixed state in dimension $d$.
The **von Neumann entropy**

$$
S(\rho)=-\operatorname{Tr}(\rho\log_2\rho)
$$

measures uncertainty in bits when the logarithm is base two. It is zero for pure
states and maximal for the maximally mixed state. Entropy becomes a diagnostic of
entanglement when applied to reduced states: the Bell state is pure globally, yet
its one-qubit reductions have one bit of entropy.

### Born rule and measured information

Measurement is the bridge between the quantum and classical worlds. The **Born
rule** assigns probabilities to outcomes. For projective measurement in the
basis $\{|k\rangle\}$,

$$
p(k)=\langle k|\rho|k\rangle.
$$

For an observable $O$, the expectation value is

$$
\langle O\rangle=\operatorname{Tr}(O\rho).
$$

A simulator can often compute this expectation directly from $\rho$ or
$|\psi\rangle$. Hardware cannot reveal an expectation value in a single run. It
must be repeatedly prepared, measured, and statistically estimated. With $N$
shots, uncertainty typically scales as $1/\sqrt{N}$, and real devices add
state-preparation and measurement error. AI models trained on noiseless simulated
expectations must therefore be stress-tested against finite-shot and device-noise
conditions before being interpreted as hardware-ready.

### Fidelity as operational closeness

The **fidelity** between states quantifies closeness in a way that respects
quantum geometry. For a pure state $|\psi\rangle$ and a density matrix $\rho$,

$$
F(|\psi\rangle,\rho)=\langle\psi|\rho|\psi\rangle.
$$

For two mixed states the definition uses matrix square roots. Fidelity is used in
state preparation, benchmarking, variational optimisation, and error mitigation.
It should not be confused with algorithmic advantage: high fidelity of a small
state preparation is an engineering metric, whereas quantum advantage is a claim
about computational scaling and evidence against classical alternatives.

## From theory to code

The [`aiquantum.linalg`](../api.md) module provides the primitives introduced
here: the Pauli basis, fidelity, purity, and von Neumann entropy. Treat these as
offline reference utilities: they operate on explicit matrices in classical
memory and are therefore best suited to small systems, validation tests, and
post-processing.

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.linalg import purity, von_neumann_entropy

rho_pure = np.array([[1, 0], [0, 0]], dtype=complex)
print(purity(rho_pure))            # 1.0  -> a pure state
print(von_neumann_entropy(rho_pure))  # 0.0  -> no uncertainty

rho_mixed = np.eye(2) / 2
print(purity(rho_mixed))           # 0.5  -> maximally mixed
print(von_neumann_entropy(rho_mixed))  # 1.0 bit
```

Fidelity is the same kind of matrix-level diagnostic:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.linalg import fidelity

ket0 = np.array([[1, 0], [0, 0]], dtype=complex)
ket_plus = np.array([[0.5, 0.5], [0.5, 0.5]], dtype=complex)
print(fidelity(ket0, ket_plus))  # overlap between |0> and |+>
```

A Bell state can be built and inspected in Qiskit as a state-vector simulation:

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector, partial_trace

qc = QuantumCircuit(2)
qc.h(0)
qc.cx(0, 1)

state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)       # classical simulation
rho_a = partial_trace(state, [1])              # trace out qubit 1
print(state.probabilities_dict())              # {'00': 0.5, '11': 0.5}
print(rho_a.data)                              # approximately I / 2
```

The same conceptual circuit in Cirq emphasises explicit qubits and moments:

```python
import cirq

q0, q1 = cirq.LineQubit.range(2)
circuit = cirq.Circuit(cirq.H(q0), cirq.CNOT(q0, q1))
result = cirq.Simulator().simulate(circuit)    # classical simulation
print(circuit)
print(result.final_state_vector)
```

On real hardware, the state vector would not be printed. One would submit the
circuit, collect bit-string counts, calibrate measurement errors where possible,
and infer properties statistically. The distinction is essential: simulators are
transparent mathematical objects; processors are sampling devices.

PennyLane expresses expectation values in the hybrid-programming style used by
variational algorithms:

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as pnp

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=1)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def z_expectation(theta):
    qml.RY(theta, wires=0)
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliZ(0))

angle = pnp.array(0.25, requires_grad=True)
print(z_expectation(angle))
```

Here `default.qubit` is again a simulator. Replacing it with hardware changes the
statistical character of the result but not the underlying Born-rule definition.

## Summary

Quantum mechanics, read as information science, hands us a precise vocabulary —
states, operators, measurements, entropy — for the rest of the book. Hilbert
space carries amplitudes, tensor products create entanglement, density matrices
represent open and uncertain systems, and the Born rule converts quantum
structure into classical data. The density matrix is our universal description of
real quantum hardware, and its exponential dimensionality is exactly the pressure
point at which machine learning becomes indispensable. Throughout the book, keep
separate the exact objects manipulated by small classical simulations and the
sampled, noisy evidence returned by physical devices.
