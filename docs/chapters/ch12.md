## Motivation

The quantum many-body problem — predicting the behaviour of strongly correlated
electrons and spins — is the grand challenge underlying high-temperature
superconductivity, quantum magnetism, and topological matter. Its difficulty is
the exponential growth of Hilbert space, and its tractability, where it exists,
comes from a single fact: physical ground states are not generic. They are
weakly entangled, and that structure can be exploited by tensor networks and,
increasingly, by neural networks.

## Theory

A general state of $n$ spins needs $2^n$ amplitudes, but ground states of local,
gapped Hamiltonians obey an **area law**: their entanglement entropy scales with
the boundary, not the volume, of a region. This is the licence to compress.

**Tensor networks** make the compression explicit. The matrix product state (MPS)
writes each amplitude as a product of matrices, one per site, whose dimension —
the bond dimension — controls the captured entanglement. The density-matrix
renormalisation group (DMRG) optimises MPS efficiently and is the gold standard in
one dimension. Higher-dimensional generalisations (PEPS) and scale-invariant ones
(MERA) extend the idea, though at greater computational cost.

**Neural quantum states** (NQS) replace the tensor factorisation with a neural
network: the amplitude $\psi(\mathbf{s})$ of a spin configuration $\mathbf{s}$ is
the output of a network, optimised variationally to minimise the energy. Carleo
and Troyer showed that even a restricted Boltzmann machine captures the ground
states of paradigmatic models, and deeper architectures now rival or exceed
tensor networks for certain systems, particularly in two dimensions and with
volume-law entanglement that defeats MPS. The two approaches are complementary:
tensor networks offer rigorous guarantees and controlled accuracy; neural states
offer flexibility and favourable scaling for hard geometries.

A unifying theme connects this chapter to tomography (Chapter 4): in both, a
compact, structured ansatz stands in for an exponentially large object, and the
relevant question is whether the ansatz's expressive capacity matches the
entanglement of the target.

## From theory to code

The library's `linalg` entropy tools let us probe entanglement directly; the
notebook for this chapter builds an MPS and an NQS on top of them. For example,
the von Neumann entropy of a subsystem diagnoses how much bond dimension an MPS
will need:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.linalg import von_neumann_entropy

# Reduced state of one half of a Bell pair: maximally entangled -> 1 bit.
reduced = np.eye(2) / 2
print(von_neumann_entropy(reduced))   # 1.0
```

## Summary

The area law is the structural fact that rescues the many-body problem. Tensor
networks and neural quantum states are two compressions of the same object, each
matched to a different entanglement regime.
