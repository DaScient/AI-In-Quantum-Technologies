## Motivation

The quantum many-body problem — predicting the behaviour of strongly correlated electrons and spins — is the grand challenge underlying high-temperature superconductivity, quantum magnetism, topological matter, and parts of quantum chemistry. Its difficulty is the exponential growth of Hilbert space. A chain of $n$ spin-$1/2$ particles has $2^n$ complex amplitudes; a two-dimensional lattice reaches impossible dimensions even faster.

Yet condensed-matter physics exists because nature rarely explores an arbitrary vector in Hilbert space. Ground states of local Hamiltonians, low-energy excitations, thermal states at moderate correlation length, and states generated by shallow circuits have structure. They exhibit locality, symmetry, and constrained **entanglement**. Tensor networks and neural quantum states exploit that structure by replacing an exponentially large table of amplitudes with a compact ansatz.

This chapter is the conceptual hinge between many-body physics and AI. Tensor networks compress by factorising tensors according to geometry. Neural quantum states compress by learning an amplitude function. Both are variational models; both succeed when their inductive bias matches the target state; both fail when the entanglement or sign structure exceeds what the ansatz can efficiently express.

## Theory

### Hilbert-space growth and locality

A pure state of $n$ spins can be written

$$
|\psi\rangle = \sum_{s_1,\ldots,s_n\in\{0,1\}} \psi(s_1,\ldots,s_n)|s_1\ldots s_n\rangle.
$$

The amplitude tensor $\psi(s_1,\ldots,s_n)$ has $2^n$ entries. Generic states therefore require exponential memory. Physical Hamiltonians, however, are usually **local**:

$$
\hat{H}=\sum_i h_{i,i+1} + \sum_i h_i,
$$

or have interactions that decay with distance. Locality constrains correlations. The ground state of a gapped one-dimensional local Hamiltonian obeys an **area law**: the entanglement entropy of a region scales with the size of its boundary, not with its volume. In one dimension the boundary is just the cut between sites, so the entropy is bounded independently of subsystem length.

The relevant diagnostic is the von Neumann entropy of a reduced density matrix. For a bipartition $A|B$, form $\rho_A=\mathrm{Tr}_B |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$ and compute

$$
S(A)=-\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_A\log_2\rho_A).
$$

If $S(A)$ is small across every cut, the state admits a compact one-dimensional tensor-network representation. If $S(A)$ grows linearly with subsystem size, a small matrix product state cannot represent it faithfully.

### Matrix product states

A **matrix product state** (MPS) factorises the amplitude tensor into local tensors:

$$
\psi(s_1,\ldots,s_n)=\sum_{\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_{n-1}} A^{s_1}_{\alpha_1}A^{s_2}_{\alpha_1\alpha_2}\cdots A^{s_n}_{\alpha_{n-1}}.
$$

The internal indices $\alpha_i$ have dimension at most $D$, the **bond dimension**. Increasing $D$ increases expressivity and computational cost. Across any cut, an MPS has Schmidt rank at most $D$, implying

$$
S \leq \log_2 D.
$$

This inequality gives an engineering rule of thumb: every additional bit of entanglement roughly doubles the bond dimension required for exact representation.

```text
physical indices:     s1        s2        s3                 sn
                      |         |         |                  |
MPS tensors:        [A1]--a1--[A2]--a2--[A3]-- ... --a(n-1)--[An]
                    left     internal bond indices             right
```

The density-matrix renormalisation group (DMRG) optimises an MPS variationally by sweeping along the chain, updating one or two tensors at a time while the rest of the network acts as an environment. In one dimension it is the gold standard because the ansatz matches the entanglement structure of many ground states and the optimisation is highly controlled.

### Beyond one dimension: PEPS, MERA, and cost

Higher-dimensional systems require richer networks. **Projected entangled-pair states** (PEPS) place tensors on a two-dimensional lattice and connect neighbouring bonds. They naturally encode a two-dimensional area law, but exact contraction is computationally hard, so practical algorithms use approximations. **MERA**, the multi-scale entanglement renormalisation ansatz, adds disentanglers and is well suited to critical systems where correlations persist across scales.

The trade-off is familiar from numerical engineering: a representation aligned with geometry reduces the number of parameters, but the solver becomes more complex. MPS has efficient contraction in one dimension. PEPS better matches two-dimensional area laws but pays a contraction cost. MERA exposes scale structure but requires specialised optimisation. Choosing the ansatz is therefore a modelling decision, not a purely computational one.

### Neural quantum states

**Neural quantum states** (NQS) replace explicit tensor factorisation with a trainable function $\psi_\theta(\mathbf{s})$. Given a spin configuration $\mathbf{s}$, the network returns an amplitude, a log-amplitude, or separate magnitude and phase. The parameters are optimised variationally to minimise

$$
E(\theta)=\frac{\langle\psi_\theta|\hat{H}|\psi_\theta\rangle}{\langle\psi_\theta|\psi_\theta\rangle}.
$$

Monte Carlo sampling estimates observables from configurations distributed according to $|\psi_\theta(\mathbf{s})|^2$. Restricted Boltzmann machines, convolutional networks, transformers, and autoregressive models have all been used. Neural ansätze can be flexible on frustrated or two-dimensional systems, but they introduce optimisation and sampling challenges: local minima, phase learning, symmetry enforcement, and variance control.

Tensor networks and NQS are complementary:

- Tensor networks offer explicit entanglement control and, in one dimension, rigorous convergence diagnostics.
- Neural states offer architectural flexibility and can encode nonlocal patterns through depth, attention, or autoregressive ordering.
- Hybrid models combine tensor-network structure with neural corrections or use neural networks to choose tensor updates.

### Entanglement, tomography, and learnability

A unifying theme connects this chapter to tomography (Chapter 4): in both, a compact, structured ansatz stands in for an exponentially large object. Tomography reconstructs a state from measurements; variational many-body physics finds a low-energy state from a Hamiltonian. In both cases the decisive question is whether the ansatz class contains a good approximation to the target with feasible data and compute.

Entanglement is the compression budget. Low entanglement allows a small bond dimension. Symmetry reduces the effective search space. Locality makes contractions and measurements manageable. Conversely, real-time evolution after a quench can generate volume-law entanglement, causing tensor-network simulations to fail even when the Hamiltonian is simple.

### Classical simulation versus quantum hardware

Tensor networks and neural quantum states are classical algorithms running on classical hardware. They simulate quantum systems by exploiting structure. A quantum processor represents quantum states physically, but extracting useful many-body observables still requires state preparation, measurement, error mitigation, and finite-shot statistics. Neither route automatically dominates the other. Classical tensor networks are extremely strong for one-dimensional gapped systems; quantum devices may become valuable where entanglement, dimensionality, or dynamics defeat classical compression, but current NISQ experiments remain limited.

## From theory to code

The library's `linalg` entropy tools let us probe entanglement directly; the notebook for this chapter builds an MPS and an NQS on top of them. For example, the von Neumann entropy of a subsystem diagnoses how much bond dimension an MPS will need:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.linalg import von_neumann_entropy

# Reduced state of one half of a Bell pair: maximally entangled -> 1 bit.
reduced = np.eye(2) / 2
print(von_neumann_entropy(reduced))   # 1.0
```

A toy MPS stores one tensor per site. The following sketch is deliberately small; production libraries canonicalise tensors, exploit symmetries, and optimise contractions.

```python
import numpy as np

n_sites = 4
phys_dim = 2
bond_dim = 3
mps = [np.random.normal(size=(1, phys_dim, bond_dim))]
mps += [np.random.normal(size=(bond_dim, phys_dim, bond_dim)) for _ in range(n_sites - 2)]
mps += [np.random.normal(size=(bond_dim, phys_dim, 1))]

print([tensor.shape for tensor in mps])
```

A Cirq circuit can generate entanglement for comparison, but simulating the circuit below on a laptop is still classical simulation. Executing it on hardware would add device topology, calibration, decoherence, and shot noise.

```python
import cirq

qubits = cirq.LineQubit.range(4)
circuit = cirq.Circuit(
    cirq.H(qubits[0]),
    cirq.CNOT(qubits[0], qubits[1]),
    cirq.CNOT(qubits[1], qubits[2]),
    cirq.CNOT(qubits[2], qubits[3]),
)
print(circuit)
```

## Summary

The area law is the structural fact that rescues large parts of the many-body problem. Tensor networks and neural quantum states are two compressions of the same exponentially large amplitude tensor, each matched to a different entanglement and geometry regime. Entropy diagnoses representability; bond dimension buys entanglement; optimisation turns the ansatz into physics.
