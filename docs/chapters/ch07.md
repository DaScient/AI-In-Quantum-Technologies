## Motivation

A quantum computer's gates are not abstractions; they are physical pulses — shaped microwave, radio-frequency, optical, or laser fields — applied to a Hamiltonian. Finding the pulse that realises a desired gate, quickly and robustly against noise, is **quantum optimal control**, one of the earliest and most successful arenas for optimisation and learning in quantum technology.

The engineering problem is familiar: drive a system from one state to another while obeying actuator limits, bandwidth limits, and uncertainty in the plant. The quantum version is sharper because the state is complex-valued, evolution is unitary when closed, and measurement usually cannot be used continuously without disturbing the dynamics. A superconducting transmon, trapped ion, spin qubit, or neutral atom is controlled by waveforms that are digitised by arbitrary waveform generators, distorted by room-temperature and cryogenic electronics, and finally experienced by a device whose Hamiltonian parameters drift in time.

Optimal control bridges the gap between a logical instruction such as “apply an $X$ gate” and a calibrated waveform. Classical simulation is the design environment: matrix exponentials, differential-equation solvers, and gradient calculations predict candidate pulses. Real quantum hardware then supplies validation and calibration data, including shot noise, decoherence, crosstalk, leakage, waveform distortion, and imperfect state preparation and measurement. Claims about pulse quality therefore mean different things depending on the setting: high simulated fidelity is a necessary design signal, not a guarantee of hardware performance.

## Theory

### Time-dependent Hamiltonians

Consider a finite-dimensional quantum system whose Hamiltonian is steered by externally controllable fields $u_c(t)$:

$$
H(t) = H_\mathrm{drift} + \sum_c u_c(t) H_c.
$$

The **drift Hamiltonian** $H_\mathrm{drift}$ contains terms always present: qubit transition frequencies, static couplings, anharmonicities, detunings, and unwanted interactions. The **control Hamiltonians** $H_c$ describe directions in Hilbert space that the laboratory can actuate. For a single qubit in a rotating frame, $H_x \propto X$ and $H_y \propto Y$ correspond to the in-phase and quadrature components of a microwave drive. In a multi-qubit device, controls may also include tunable couplers, local detunings, or laser amplitudes.

The Schrödinger equation for the propagator is

$$
\frac{dU(t)}{dt} = -i H(t) U(t), \qquad U(0)=I,
$$

with $\hbar=1$. For closed-system gate design, the goal is to choose $u_c(t)$ so that $U(T)$ approximates a desired unitary $U_\mathrm{target}$ at final time $T$. For open systems one replaces $U$ by a quantum channel or density-matrix evolution under a Lindblad master equation, but the optimisation structure is similar.

Digital control hardware motivates a piecewise-constant approximation. Divide $T$ into $N$ intervals of duration $\Delta t$. On interval $k$,

$$
H_k = H_\mathrm{drift} + \sum_c u_{k,c} H_c,
$$

and

$$
U_k = \exp(-i H_k \Delta t), \qquad U(T)=U_N U_{N-1}\cdots U_1.
$$

The ordering matters: the rightmost pulse acts first. This product is the discrete analogue of the time-ordered exponential. It is also the object that makes optimal control computationally tractable: the variables are the amplitudes $u_{k,c}$, not an arbitrary continuous function.

### The control problem

A complete pulse-design problem is not simply “maximise fidelity”. A realistic objective may combine:

- **Gate accuracy**: closeness to $U_\mathrm{target}$ or to a target channel.
- **Duration**: shorter gates reduce exposure to decoherence but may require stronger drives.
- **Energy and amplitude limits**: waveforms must remain within hardware-safe ranges.
- **Bandwidth constraints**: pulses should not contain frequency components the control chain cannot deliver.
- **Leakage suppression**: population should remain in the computational subspace.
- **Robustness**: performance should vary slowly under detuning, calibration error, or model uncertainty.

A common closed-system fidelity for a $d$-dimensional unitary is

$$
F = \frac{1}{d^2}\left|\operatorname{Tr}\!\left[U_\mathrm{target}^\dagger U(T)\right]\right|^2.
$$

This expression is insensitive to global phase: $U$ and $e^{i\theta}U$ implement the same gate. The corresponding loss is often $1-F$ plus regularisers, for example

$$
\mathcal{L} = 1-F + \lambda \sum_{k,c} u_{k,c}^2 + \eta \sum_{k,c} (u_{k+1,c}-u_{k,c})^2.
$$

The first penalty discourages high power; the second discourages rapidly varying controls. Neither term is “quantum” in itself, but both are essential for pulses that can be deployed outside a simulator.

### GRAPE and the analytic gradient

**GRAPE** (Gradient Ascent Pulse Engineering) is the canonical gradient method for piecewise-constant quantum control. Its efficiency comes from differentiating the product of propagators without recomputing the whole evolution for every control amplitude.

Let

$$
F_k = U_k U_{k-1}\cdots U_1
$$

be the forward product up to interval $k$, and

$$
B_k = U_N U_{N-1}\cdots U_k
$$

be the backward product from interval $k$ to the end. If only the amplitude $u_{k,c}$ changes, only $U_k$ changes. The derivative of the final unitary is

$$
\frac{\partial U(T)}{\partial u_{k,c}}
= U_N\cdots U_{k+1}
\frac{\partial U_k}{\partial u_{k,c}}
U_{k-1}\cdots U_1.
$$

For small $\Delta t$, a first-order derivative is

$$
\frac{\partial U_k}{\partial u_{k,c}}
\approx -i\Delta t\, H_c U_k,
$$

with more accurate expressions available from exact matrix-exponential derivatives. Define

$$
\Phi = \operatorname{Tr}\!\left[U_\mathrm{target}^\dagger U(T)\right].
$$

Since $F=|\Phi|^2/d^2$,

$$
\frac{\partial F}{\partial u_{k,c}}
= \frac{2}{d^2}\operatorname{Re}\!\left[
\Phi^*\operatorname{Tr}\!\left(
U_\mathrm{target}^\dagger
\frac{\partial U(T)}{\partial u_{k,c}}
\right)
\right].
$$

Forward and backward caches make all gradients available after one forward sweep and one backward sweep. The complexity is linear in the number of time slices, up to the cost of matrix exponentials and multiplications. This is why GRAPE remains a workhorse: it uses exact structure rather than estimating every derivative by finite differences.

A pulse-optimisation loop looks like this:

```text
initial pulse u[k,c]
        |
        v
+-------------------+       +---------------------+
| simulate dynamics | ----> | compute infidelity  |
| U_N ... U_1       |       | and penalties        |
+-------------------+       +---------------------+
        |                             |
        v                             v
+-------------------+       +---------------------+
| cache forward and | ----> | analytic gradient   |
| backward products |       | dL/du[k,c]          |
+-------------------+       +---------------------+
        ^                             |
        |                             v
        +--------- optimiser update <-+
                  (line search, Adam,
                   L-BFGS, constraints)
```

### Beyond GRAPE: CRAB, DRAG, and learned controllers

GRAPE assumes a discretised waveform with many independent amplitudes. **CRAB** (Chopped Random Basis) reduces the search space by expanding the pulse in a small randomised basis, often sinusoidal components. This can be useful when gradients are unavailable or when one wants low-bandwidth pulses by construction.

**DRAG** (Derivative Removal by Adiabatic Gate) is especially important for weakly anharmonic superconducting qubits. It adds quadrature components related to derivatives of the main envelope to reduce leakage into non-computational levels. DRAG is not a generic optimiser; it is a physics-informed pulse ansatz that can be tuned analytically or numerically.

**Reinforcement learning** frames pulse optimisation as sequential decision-making. An agent chooses amplitudes or pulse parameters, receives a reward based on simulated or measured performance, and improves its policy. This is an option when analytic gradients are unavailable, when the model is partly unknown, or when the objective includes discrete calibration choices. It should not be presented as automatically superior: RL can be sample hungry, sensitive to reward design, and constrained by the fidelity of its simulator. On hardware, every policy evaluation consumes experimental time and is affected by drift and shot noise.

**Differentiable simulation** provides a middle path. If the simulator is written in an automatic-differentiation framework, gradients can flow through ODE solvers, noise models, and post-processing. This enables rich losses, but the same caveat remains: differentiating an inaccurate simulator gives precise gradients for the wrong physical model.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.quantum.optimal_control` implements GRAPE with the analytic gradient and backs `/api/v1/quantum/optimal_control`:

```python
from aiquantum.linalg import X
from aiquantum.quantum import grape_pulse

result = grape_pulse(X, n_steps=20, dt=0.2, iterations=400)
print(round(result.fidelity, 4))   # -> ~1.0, a learned X gate
print(result.controls.shape)        # (20, 2): amplitudes for [X, Y] controls
```

The `aiquantum` example is the offline reference implementation: it optimises a simulated pulse for a target gate. Running such a script is not the same as applying the pulse to hardware. A device experiment would add calibration, waveform upload, repeated circuit execution, and statistical estimation of gate quality.

### PennyLane pulse sketch

PennyLane's pulse interface can express time-dependent Hamiltonians and optimise pulse parameters with differentiable programming. The following sketch is illustrative; exact device support and differentiability depend on the installed PennyLane version and backend.

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as np

coeffs = [lambda p, t: p[0] * np.cos(p[1] * t)]
ops = [qml.PauliX(0)]
H = qml.pulse.ParametrizedHamiltonian(coeffs, ops)

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=1)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def driven_qubit(params):
    qml.evolve(H)(params, t=[0.0, 1.0])
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliZ(0))

params = np.array([1.0, np.pi], requires_grad=True)
grad = qml.grad(driven_qubit)(params)
print(grad)
```

For gate objectives one typically compares the evolved unitary or process to a target rather than only measuring $\langle Z\rangle$. The important connection is that the same control amplitudes appearing in $H(t)$ become trainable parameters in a classical optimiser.

### Parameter-shift control sketch

When controls are represented as circuit rotations rather than continuous pulses, the **parameter-shift rule** gives exact gradients for many gates:

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as np

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=1)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def pulse_ansatz(theta):
    for angle in theta:
        qml.RX(angle, wires=0)
        qml.RY(0.25 * angle, wires=0)
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliX(0))

theta = np.array([0.1, 0.2, 0.3], requires_grad=True)
print(qml.grad(pulse_ansatz)(theta))
```

This is not a replacement for Hamiltonian-level pulse design, but it is a useful abstraction for teaching and for variational compilation.

## Summary

Optimal control turns gate design into constrained optimisation over time-dependent Hamiltonians. GRAPE exploits analytic gradients through cached propagator products, making high-dimensional pulse updates efficient in simulation. CRAB, DRAG, differentiable simulation, and reinforcement learning broaden the design space, but their value depends on the device model, calibration workflow, and hardware constraints. Simulated fidelity is a design signal; hardware validation must account for decoherence, leakage, crosstalk, bandwidth limits, and shot noise.
