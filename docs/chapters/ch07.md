## Motivation

A quantum computer's gates are not abstractions; they are physical pulses —
shaped microwave or laser fields — applied to a Hamiltonian. Finding the pulse
that realises a desired gate, quickly and robustly against noise, is **quantum
optimal control**, one of the earliest and most successful arenas for
optimisation and learning in quantum technology.

## Theory

Consider a system whose Hamiltonian we steer through time-dependent controls
$u_c(t)$:

$$
H(t) = H_\mathrm{drift} + \sum_c u_c(t)\, H_c.
$$

Discretising time into $N$ piecewise-constant intervals of length $\Delta t$, the
total propagator is the ordered product $U = U_N \cdots U_1$ with $U_k =
\exp(-i H_k \Delta t)$. We seek controls that maximise the gate fidelity to a
target unitary $U_\mathrm{target}$:

$$
F = \frac{1}{d^2}\left|\operatorname{Tr}\!\left[U_\mathrm{target}^\dagger U\right]\right|^2.
$$

The **GRAPE** algorithm (Gradient Ascent Pulse Engineering) computes the gradient
of $F$ with respect to each control amplitude analytically. The key observation is
that the derivative of the propagator with respect to $u_{k,c}$ involves only the
single step $k$, sandwiched between the forward propagator $U_{k-1}\cdots U_1$ and
the backward propagator $U_N \cdots U_{k+1}$. Caching these partial products makes
the full gradient computable in $\mathcal{O}(N)$ matrix multiplications, and
gradient ascent then converges rapidly to a high-fidelity pulse.

GRAPE is the gradient-based end of a spectrum. At the other end,
**reinforcement learning** dispenses with analytic gradients and learns a control
policy by interacting with a simulator or device, which is advantageous when the
dynamics are uncertain or the objective is non-differentiable. Modern
*differentiable simulation* unifies the two: backpropagating through the
simulator yields exact gradients for arbitrarily complex cost functions,
including robustness penalties that suppress sensitivity to noise.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.quantum.optimal_control` implements GRAPE with the analytic gradient
and backs `/api/v1/quantum/optimal_control`:

```python
from aiquantum.linalg import X
from aiquantum.quantum import grape_pulse

result = grape_pulse(X, n_steps=20, dt=0.2, iterations=400)
print(round(result.fidelity, 4))   # -> ~1.0, a learned X gate
print(result.controls.shape)        # (20, 2): amplitudes for [X, Y] controls
```

## Summary

Optimal control turns gate design into optimisation. GRAPE's analytic gradient
makes it efficient; reinforcement learning and differentiable simulation extend
it to uncertain dynamics and rich, robustness-aware objectives.
