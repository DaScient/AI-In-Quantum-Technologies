## Motivation

Quantum chemistry is the original "killer app" of quantum computing — and one of the most fertile grounds for machine learning. Molecules are quantum objects whose stability, spectra, reaction barriers, and binding affinities are determined by electronic wavefunctions. Solving the electronic Schrödinger equation exactly scales exponentially with the number of active orbitals, yet chemists routinely need answers for thousands or millions of candidate compounds. That mismatch between physical accuracy and design throughput is precisely where AI becomes useful.

The central observation is not that chemistry is simple. It is that many chemical properties vary smoothly across molecular space once the correct symmetries are respected. A surrogate can learn the map from nuclear charges and coordinates to energies, forces, dipoles, or excitation gaps, then evaluate that map far faster than a reference electronic-structure calculation. In pharmaceutical screening, catalysis, battery electrolytes, and carbon-capture chemistry, such models turn expensive quantum calculations into reusable training data.

Quantum computers enter from a different direction. Rather than learning a classical surrogate, algorithms such as the **variational quantum eigensolver** (VQE) prepare parameterised quantum states and estimate molecular energies from measurements. Today this is primarily a research programme: small active spaces can be demonstrated on simulators and noisy devices, but chemical advantage has not been established. The practical workflow therefore combines three layers: high-quality classical chemistry to generate labels, machine learning to interpolate and search, and quantum algorithms to explore future routes for strongly correlated systems.

## Theory

### Electronic structure as an optimisation problem

For nuclei at positions $\mathbf{R}_A$ with charges $Z_A$, the non-relativistic molecular Hamiltonian contains nuclear kinetic energy, electronic kinetic energy, electron–nuclear attraction, electron–electron repulsion, and nuclear–nuclear repulsion. The **Born–Oppenheimer approximation** treats nuclei as fixed while electrons relax much faster. The electronic Hamiltonian becomes

$$
\hat{H}_e = -\sum_i \frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 - \sum_{i,A}\frac{Z_A}{|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{R}_A|} + \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{|\mathbf{r}_i-\mathbf{r}_j|} + E_{nn},
$$

where $E_{nn}=\sum_{A<B}Z_AZ_B/|\mathbf{R}_A-\mathbf{R}_B|$ is constant for a fixed geometry. The ground-state energy is the Rayleigh–Ritz minimum

$$
E_0 = \min_{|\psi\rangle}\frac{\langle\psi|\hat{H}_e|\psi\rangle}{\langle\psi|\psi\rangle}.
$$

This variational form is the bridge between chemistry, optimisation, and learning. Hartree–Fock restricts $|\psi\rangle$ to a single Slater determinant; configuration interaction, coupled cluster, and density matrix renormalisation group enlarge the ansatz; VQE uses a quantum circuit ansatz; neural wavefunction methods use trainable functions. In every case, a high-dimensional object is replaced by a structured family with adjustable parameters.

### From orbitals to qubits

In a finite orbital basis the Hamiltonian is usually written in **second quantization**:

$$
\hat{H}=\sum_{pq} h_{pq} a_p^\dagger a_q + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{pqrs} h_{pqrs} a_p^\dagger a_q^\dagger a_r a_s.
$$

The operators $a_p^\dagger$ and $a_p$ create and annihilate electrons in spin-orbitals and obey fermionic anticommutation rules. A quantum computer stores qubits, not fermions, so a mapping is required. The **Jordan–Wigner transform** encodes orbital occupation directly and attaches a string of $Z$ operators to preserve parity:

$$
a_p^\dagger = \left(\prod_{j<p} Z_j\right)\frac{X_p-iY_p}{2},\qquad
a_p = \left(\prod_{j<p} Z_j\right)\frac{X_p+iY_p}{2}.
$$

Other mappings, including Bravyi–Kitaev and parity encodings, trade locality against parity bookkeeping. After mapping, the molecular Hamiltonian becomes a weighted sum of Pauli strings, $\hat{H}=\sum_k c_k P_k$. A VQE loop prepares $|\psi(\boldsymbol{\theta})\rangle$, estimates each $\langle P_k\rangle$ from measurement shots, and uses a classical optimiser to update $\boldsymbol{\theta}$.

```text
geometry -> integrals -> fermionic Hamiltonian -> qubit mapping -> ansatz circuit
   ^                                                                  |
   |                                                                  v
 classical optimiser <- energy estimate <- Pauli measurements <- quantum device/simulator
```

On a classical simulator this pipeline is deterministic up to numerical choices and scales exponentially with qubit count. On real hardware it is affected by finite shots, state-preparation errors, decoherence, readout noise, and circuit compilation. The distinction matters: a plotted H$_2$ dissociation curve from a simulator validates modelling assumptions, whereas a hardware experiment also tests calibration, noise mitigation, and measurement allocation.

### Learning molecular structure–property maps

The electronic ground-state energy is a functional of nuclear charges and positions. A learning approach replaces the expensive solver with a regressor $E \approx f_\theta(\text{descriptor})$. The descriptor must respect physical invariances:

- **Translation invariance:** moving the whole molecule cannot change its energy.
- **Rotation invariance:** choosing a different laboratory frame cannot change scalar properties.
- **Permutation invariance:** exchanging identical atoms cannot change observables.
- **Size extensivity:** for many properties, two distant subsystems should contribute additively.

A classic descriptor is the **Coulomb matrix**,

$$
M_{ij} = \begin{cases} \tfrac12 Z_i^{2.4} & i = j \\ \dfrac{Z_i Z_j}{\lVert
\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j \rVert} & i \neq j \end{cases},
$$

which is translation- and rotation-invariant by construction. Sorting its eigenvalues yields a fixed-length, permutation-invariant fingerprint suitable for a **kernel-ridge regressor** with a Gaussian kernel. In kernel ridge regression, predictions are weighted sums of similarities to training molecules:

$$
\hat{E}(x)=\sum_{m=1}^{N}\alpha_m\exp\left(-\frac{\lVert x-x_m\rVert^2}{2\sigma^2}\right),
$$

with coefficients $\boldsymbol{\alpha}=(K+\lambda I)^{-1}\mathbf{y}$. The model is transparent: nearby molecules in descriptor space have similar energies, and regularisation controls overfitting.

### Equivariant networks and learned potentials

Descriptor models encode invariance before learning. Modern neural potentials often encode the stronger property of **equivariance**: internal vector or tensor features rotate in the same way as the molecule. **SE(3)-equivariant** neural networks operate directly on atomic coordinates, message between atoms inside a cutoff radius, and produce energies whose gradients give forces. This is essential for molecular dynamics because a small non-conservative force error can accumulate into unphysical trajectories.

The engineering analogy is finite-element simulation with a learned constitutive law. The mesh geometry should not depend on how the engineer rotates the part; likewise, a molecular network should not waste data relearning the same bond under every orientation. Equivariance builds the coordinate system into the architecture, improving data efficiency and extrapolation.

### H$_2$ dissociation as a minimal benchmark

Hydrogen dissociation is simple enough to plot and subtle enough to teach correlation. Near equilibrium, a single-reference wavefunction works well. At large separation, the two electrons localise on different atoms and a single determinant becomes qualitatively wrong. A good method should approach the correct separated-atom limit without inventing spurious charge transfer. For VQE, H$_2$ is the canonical first example because a small active space maps to only a few qubits; for ML, it is a reminder that training data must cover stretched geometries if the model is expected to describe bond breaking.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.qchem` provides the Coulomb-matrix descriptor and the kernel-ridge regressor, and a Morse-potential model of the H$_2$ dissociation curve used to validate variational solvers:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.qchem import coulomb_matrix, h2_dissociation_curve

cm = coulomb_matrix(np.array([1.0, 1.0]),
                    np.array([[0, 0, 0], [0, 0, 0.74]]))
curve = h2_dissociation_curve(np.linspace(0.4, 3.0, 100))
print(curve.min())   # binding energy at equilibrium (Hartree)
```

The descriptor backs `/api/v1/qchem/ground_state`.

A minimal PennyLane-style VQE expresses the same variational principle. This static example omits integral generation and uses a toy two-qubit Hamiltonian; production workflows build the Pauli coefficients from a chemistry package and then validate against classical reference data.

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as pnp

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=4, shots=None)
H = qml.Hamiltonian([-1.05, 0.39, -0.39], [qml.Identity(0), qml.PauliZ(0), qml.PauliZ(1)])

@qml.qnode(dev)
def energy(theta):
    qml.BasisState(pnp.array([1, 1, 0, 0]), wires=range(4))
    qml.DoubleExcitation(theta, wires=[0, 1, 2, 3])
    return qml.expval(H)
```

The snippet is a conceptual ladder, not a claim of hardware advantage. Running it on `default.qubit` is classical simulation. Running an analogous circuit on a quantum processor would require finite-shot estimation, transpilation to the device topology, and error analysis.

## Summary

The structure-to-property map is smooth and learnable when molecular symmetries are respected. Invariant descriptors with kernel ridge regression form a transparent baseline; equivariant networks improve accuracy and force consistency; variational quantum solvers connect the electronic Hamiltonian to circuits. Classical simulation, ML surrogates, and real quantum hardware answer different questions and must not be conflated.
