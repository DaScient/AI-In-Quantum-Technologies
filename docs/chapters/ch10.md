## Learning objectives

- Frame electronic-structure prediction as a learning problem.
- Construct permutation-invariant molecular descriptors.
- Use a kernel-ridge regressor as a transparent baseline for energy prediction.

## Motivation

Quantum chemistry is the original "killer app" of quantum computing — and one of
the most fertile grounds for machine learning. Solving the electronic
Schrödinger equation exactly scales exponentially, but the *map* from molecular
structure to property is smooth and learnable. Surrogate models trained on
reference calculations now predict energies and spectra in milliseconds, enabling
high-throughput screening and inverse molecular design.

## Theory

The electronic ground-state energy is a functional of the nuclear charges and
positions. A learning approach replaces the expensive solver with a regressor
$E \approx f_\theta(\text{descriptor})$. The descriptor must respect the physical
invariances: the energy is unchanged under translation, rotation, and permutation
of identical atoms. A classic choice is the **Coulomb matrix**,

$$
M_{ij} = \begin{cases} \tfrac12 Z_i^{2.4} & i = j \\ \dfrac{Z_i Z_j}{\lVert
\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j \rVert} & i \neq j \end{cases},
$$

which is translation- and rotation-invariant by construction. Sorting its
eigenvalues yields a fixed-length, permutation-invariant fingerprint suitable for
a **kernel-ridge regressor** with a Gaussian kernel. This baseline, due to Rupp
and co-workers, already predicts atomisation energies to chemical accuracy on
datasets such as QM9.

State-of-the-art models go further by building symmetry into the architecture.
**SE(3)-equivariant** neural networks — the family to which the textbook's
`MolQNet` belongs — operate directly on atomic coordinates and transform
correctly under rotations, achieving higher accuracy and better data efficiency
than descriptor-based methods. On the quantum-hardware side, the **variational
quantum eigensolver** (Chapter 17) estimates ground-state energies on near-term
devices, and ML assists by reducing measurement overhead and mitigating noise.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.qchem` provides the Coulomb-matrix descriptor and the kernel-ridge
regressor, and a Morse-potential model of the H$_2$ dissociation curve used to
validate variational solvers:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.qchem import coulomb_matrix, h2_dissociation_curve

cm = coulomb_matrix(np.array([1.0, 1.0]),
                    np.array([[0, 0, 0], [0, 0, 0.74]]))
curve = h2_dissociation_curve(np.linspace(0.4, 3.0, 100))
print(curve.min())   # binding energy at equilibrium (Hartree)
```

The descriptor backs `/api/v1/qchem/ground_state`.

## Summary

The structure-to-property map is smooth and learnable. Invariant descriptors with
kernel ridge regression form a transparent baseline; equivariant networks and
variational quantum solvers push accuracy and scale.
