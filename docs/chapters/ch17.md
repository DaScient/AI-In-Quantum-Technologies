## Motivation

**Variational quantum algorithms** (VQAs) are the leading strategy for extracting
value from noisy, intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) hardware. They place a
parameterised quantum circuit in a feedback loop with a classical optimiser, using
the quantum device only for what it does best — preparing and measuring states —
while the optimisation runs classically. The variational quantum eigensolver
(VQE) and the quantum approximate optimisation algorithm (QAOA) are the flagship
instances.

## Theory

A VQA defines a parameterised state $|\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)\rangle = U(\boldsymbol
\theta)|0\rangle$ and a cost function that is the expectation of some observable,
$C(\boldsymbol\theta) = \langle\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)|H|\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)
\rangle$. For VQE, $H$ is a molecular Hamiltonian and $C$ its energy, minimised to
approximate the ground state (Chapter 10). For QAOA, $H$ encodes a combinatorial
objective. The quantum computer evaluates $C$ and its gradient — via the
parameter-shift rule, which expresses derivatives exactly as differences of
circuit evaluations — and a classical optimiser updates $\boldsymbol\theta$.

The central obstacle is the **barren plateau**: for many random, deep, or
globally-cost ansätze, the gradient vanishes exponentially in the number of qubits,
so the optimiser wanders a flat landscape and learning stalls. Understanding when
plateaus arise — and avoiding them — is the dominant theoretical question of the
field. Mitigations include shallow and structured ansätze that respect problem
symmetry, local rather than global cost functions, careful initialisation
(identity-block strategies), and layer-wise training. These design principles echo
the inductive-bias lessons of Chapter 2: structure, not brute force, makes
optimisation tractable.

VQAs are hybrid by nature, and their classical inner loop is ordinary
optimisation. The repository's kernel-based trainer illustrates the
classical-optimisation half of such a loop in a transparent, fully runnable
setting, standing in for the device evaluation during development.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.qml.train` solves the classical training problem for a quantum kernel
model, backing `/api/v1/qml/train`:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.qml import train_hybrid_model

x = np.array([[0.0], [0.1], [1.5], [1.6]])
y = np.array([0, 0, 1, 1])
summary = train_hybrid_model(x, y)
print(summary["train_accuracy"])
```

## Summary

VQAs pair a parameterised circuit with a classical optimiser, the dominant NISQ
paradigm. Barren plateaus are their central pitfall, tamed by structured,
shallow, symmetry-aware ansätze and disciplined training.
