## Motivation

**Variational quantum algorithms** (VQAs) are the leading strategy for extracting value from noisy, intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) hardware. They place a parameterised quantum circuit in a feedback loop with a classical optimiser, using the quantum device only for what it does best — preparing and measuring states — while the optimisation runs classically. The variational quantum eigensolver (VQE) and the quantum approximate optimisation algorithm (QAOA) are the flagship instances.

This division of labour is pragmatic. Fully fault-tolerant algorithms such as phase estimation may require circuit depths far beyond current devices, whereas shallow circuits can be executed today, albeit noisily. VQAs trade mathematical guarantees for experimental feasibility: rather than implementing a long coherent algorithm, they repeatedly evaluate a short circuit, estimate a cost, and adjust parameters. The approach resembles training a small neural network whose forward pass happens on a quantum processing unit.

The promise is matched by difficulty. Optimisation can be unstable, gradients can vanish, hardware noise can distort the objective, and the number of measurements can grow quickly. VQAs are therefore not a magic NISQ advantage engine. They are a disciplined template for asking whether a structured quantum ansatz, paired with a classical optimiser, can solve a task better than known classical or purely simulated alternatives.

## Theory

### Parameterised quantum circuits and ansätze

A **parameterised quantum circuit** (PQC) prepares

$$
|\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)\rangle = U(\boldsymbol\theta)|0\rangle^{\otimes n},
$$

where $\boldsymbol\theta$ are tunable angles or phases. The circuit architecture is called an **ansatz**: an educated guess about the family of states likely to contain a good solution. Hardware-efficient ansätze use native gates and connectivity to keep circuits shallow. Problem-inspired ansätze encode chemistry, spin, graph, or symmetry structure. The distinction matters because trainability depends as much on the ansatz as on the optimiser.

A typical ansatz alternates trainable single-qubit rotations with entangling layers:

```text
q0: ──Ry(θ0)──Rz(θ1)──●────────Ry(θ4)──Rz(θ5)──●────
                      │                         │
q1: ──Ry(θ2)──Rz(θ3)──X────────Ry(θ6)──Rz(θ7)──X────
```

This circuit is easy to run, but not automatically useful. If the task has particle-number conservation, translational symmetry, or locality, an ansatz that violates those structures may waste parameters exploring irrelevant states.

### The variational principle

The mathematical foundation of VQE is the **variational principle**. For a Hermitian Hamiltonian $H$ with ground-state energy $E_0$, every normalised state $|\psi\rangle$ satisfies

$$
E(\psi)=\langle\psi|H|\psi\rangle \ge E_0.
$$

A parameterised family therefore gives an upper bound

$$
E(\boldsymbol\theta)=\langle\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)|H|\psi(\boldsymbol\theta)\rangle,
$$

and minimising $E(\boldsymbol\theta)$ searches for the best state available inside the ansatz. In chemistry, $H$ is decomposed into measurable Pauli terms,

$$
H=\sum_j c_j P_j,
$$

so the quantum device estimates $\langle P_j\rangle$ and the classical processor forms the weighted sum. Exact state-vector simulation computes these expectations deterministically; hardware estimates them from finite shots under decoherence and readout error.

### VQE and QAOA

**VQE** targets ground-state energies and related observables. Its quality depends on whether the ansatz can represent the relevant low-energy physics and whether the optimiser can find the parameters. Chemistry-inspired ansätze can be expressive but deep; hardware-efficient ansätze are shallow but may be poorly aligned with the molecule.

**QAOA** addresses combinatorial optimisation. For a cost Hamiltonian $C$ and a mixer Hamiltonian $B$, depth $p$ QAOA prepares

$$
|\boldsymbol\gamma,\boldsymbol\beta\rangle = \prod_{\ell=1}^{p} e^{-i\beta_\ell B} e^{-i\gamma_\ell C}|+\rangle^{\otimes n}.
$$

The measured objective is the expectation of $C$. QAOA is problem-inspired: $C$ encodes the objective, while $B$ explores feasible bitstrings. At low depth it is hardware friendly but may be far from optimal; increasing depth improves expressivity but worsens noise sensitivity and optimisation difficulty.

### The variational loop

Every VQA can be viewed as the same loop with different state preparation and cost evaluation:

```text
        ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │ classical optimiser proposes θ               │
        └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                               │
                               v
        ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │ quantum device/simulator prepares U(θ)|0>     │
        │ and measures observables or bitstring samples │
        └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                               │
                               v
        ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
        │ classical post-processing estimates C(θ),     │
        │ gradients, uncertainties, and stopping tests  │
        └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                               │
                               └────── update θ ───────┘
```

The boundary is important. Classical code handles batching, caching, gradient aggregation, constraints, and optimiser state. Quantum execution supplies expectation values or samples. Latency between the two can dominate runtime, especially when many parameter-shift evaluations are required.

### Parameter-shift gradients

For a gate of the form $e^{-i\theta G}$ whose generator has two distinct eigenvalues, derivatives can often be obtained exactly from shifted circuit evaluations. For a Pauli rotation $R(\theta)=e^{-i\theta P/2}$ and cost $C(\theta)$,

$$
\frac{\partial C}{\partial \theta}
= \frac{1}{2}\left[C\left(\theta+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)-C\left(\theta-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\right].
$$

This **parameter-shift rule** is not a finite-difference approximation; for eligible gates it is exact in the noiseless mathematical model. On hardware, each shifted cost is still estimated from samples, so the gradient has statistical uncertainty. A circuit with $d$ trainable parameters may require many additional circuit evaluations per optimisation step unless gradients are batched, estimated stochastically, or replaced by gradient-free methods.

### Landscapes and barren plateaus

The central obstacle is the **barren plateau**: for many random, deep, or globally-cost ansätze, gradients concentrate near zero as the number of qubits grows. More precisely, the gradient variance can decay with system size, often exponentially under assumptions such as sufficiently expressive random circuits. An optimiser then sees a flat landscape; parameter updates become dominated by shot noise and numerical error.

Barren plateaus are not merely small gradients in a bad implementation. They are a statement about the geometry of high-dimensional quantum state spaces and the concentration of measure. Common risk factors include:

- Deep unstructured ansätze that approximate random unitaries.
- Global cost functions measured on many qubits at once.
- Random initialisation far from a useful region.
- Noise-induced flattening, where decoherence suppresses cost sensitivity.

Mitigations are design choices, not afterthoughts: shallow circuits, local costs, symmetry-preserving ansätze, identity-block initialisation, layer-wise training, parameter tying, and problem-informed initial parameters. These echo the inductive-bias lessons of classical machine learning: structure makes optimisation possible.

### Data re-uploading

In **data re-uploading**, classical data enter a PQC multiple times across layers:

$$
U(\boldsymbol\theta,x)=\prod_{\ell} W_\ell(\boldsymbol\theta_\ell) S_\ell(x),
$$

where $S_\ell(x)$ encodes data and $W_\ell$ contains trainable parameters. Re-uploading lets a small number of qubits represent nonlinear functions of classical inputs, much like adding depth to a neural network. It is useful for hybrid classifiers and regressors, but the same caveats apply: expressivity must match the task, gradients must be trainable, and classical baselines must be taken seriously.

The repository function keeps the training loop fully reproducible by using a simulated quantum-kernel model. The following snippets show how the same variational ideas appear in common frameworks.

### PennyLane VQE with parameter-shift gradients

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as np

hamiltonian = qml.Hamiltonian(
    coeffs=[-1.05, 0.39, -0.39, -0.01, 0.18],
    observables=[
        qml.Identity(0),
        qml.PauliZ(0),
        qml.PauliZ(1),
        qml.PauliZ(0) @ qml.PauliZ(1),
        qml.PauliX(0) @ qml.PauliX(1),
    ],
)

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=2, shots=None)


@qml.qnode(dev, diff_method="parameter-shift")
def energy(theta):
    qml.RY(theta[0], wires=0)
    qml.RY(theta[1], wires=1)
    qml.CNOT(wires=[0, 1])
    return qml.expval(hamiltonian)


theta = np.array([0.1, -0.2], requires_grad=True)
grad = qml.grad(energy)(theta)
print(float(energy(theta)), grad)
```

The code uses an exact simulator. Replacing it with finite-shot hardware changes the cost and gradient into estimates.

### PennyLane QAOA sketch

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as np

wires = range(3)
dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=wires, shots=2000)

cost_h = qml.PauliZ(0) @ qml.PauliZ(1) + qml.PauliZ(1) @ qml.PauliZ(2)
mixer_h = qml.PauliX(0) + qml.PauliX(1) + qml.PauliX(2)


@qml.qnode(dev, diff_method="parameter-shift")
def qaoa_cost(gamma, beta):
    for wire in wires:
        qml.Hadamard(wires=wire)
    qml.ApproxTimeEvolution(cost_h, gamma, 1)
    qml.ApproxTimeEvolution(mixer_h, beta, 1)
    return qml.expval(cost_h)


print(qaoa_cost(np.array(0.2), np.array(0.5)))
```

### Qiskit `Estimator`-based VQE sketch

```python
import numpy as np
from qiskit.circuit import QuantumCircuit, ParameterVector
from qiskit.primitives import StatevectorEstimator
from qiskit.quantum_info import SparsePauliOp
from scipy.optimize import minimize

params = ParameterVector("θ", 2)
ansatz = QuantumCircuit(2)
ansatz.ry(params[0], 0)
ansatz.ry(params[1], 1)
ansatz.cx(0, 1)

hamiltonian = SparsePauliOp.from_list([
    ("II", -1.05), ("ZI", 0.39), ("IZ", -0.39), ("ZZ", -0.01), ("XX", 0.18)
])
estimator = StatevectorEstimator()


def objective(values):
    bound = ansatz.assign_parameters(dict(zip(params, values)))
    job = estimator.run([(bound, hamiltonian)])
    return float(job.result()[0].data.evs)

result = minimize(objective, x0=np.array([0.1, -0.2]), method="COBYLA")
print(result.fun, result.x)
```

This sketch uses a state-vector primitive for clarity. A hardware-backed estimator introduces queueing, shot noise, calibration drift, and device constraints, so convergence should be interpreted experimentally rather than as proof of advantage.

## Summary

VQAs pair a parameterised circuit with a classical optimiser, the dominant NISQ paradigm. VQE follows the variational principle for Hamiltonian ground states; QAOA alternates problem and mixer evolutions for combinatorial objectives; data re-uploading uses PQCs as nonlinear models for classical data. The parameter-shift rule provides exact noiseless gradients for common gates, but hardware estimates are noisy and costly. Barren plateaus, where gradient variance decays with system size, are the central trainability pitfall and demand structured, shallow, symmetry-aware ansätze and disciplined training.
