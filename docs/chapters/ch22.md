## Motivation

AI and quantum hardware do not merely coexist; they *co-evolve*. Advances in machine learning improve the design, calibration, and operation of quantum devices, while better quantum hardware enlarges the data and the experimental substrates available to AI. Understanding this loop is essential to forecasting the field, provided the forecast is stated with care: many plausible futures remain **open** and **speculative**, and none should be described as quantum advantage unless an end-to-end advantage over the best classical baseline has been empirically or theoretically established for the task at hand.

The co-evolutionary view is useful because quantum engineering is not a single bottleneck. A superconducting, trapped-ion, neutral-atom, photonic, spin, or topological platform is a stack: materials, fabrication, cryogenics or vacuum, control electronics, pulse schedules, compilers, error mitigation, calibration, decoding, and application workloads. AI can enter at many layers, but each layer also constrains the models that can be useful. A calibration policy that ignores device drift, a pulse optimiser that violates hardware bandwidth, or a generative materials model that proposes unmanufacturable structures does not improve the system. The central question is therefore not whether AI and quantum devices interact, but which closed loops are reliable enough to become engineering practice.

## Theory

### The feedback loop as a control system

The feedback runs in both directions. **AI improves hardware** when learned models reduce uncertainty about a device, choose better interventions, or compress an expensive design search. **Quantum hardware may improve AI** when it provides access to quantum data, quantum dynamics, or computational primitives that are not efficiently reproduced classically. The first direction is already visible in several engineering workflows; the second is more conditional and, for broad machine-learning acceleration, remains an open research question.

A useful abstraction is a closed-loop system:

```text
measure device -> infer state/noise -> choose action -> apply control/design change
      ^                                                        |
      |                                                        v
      +------------------ observe new device behaviour <-------+
```

The loop contains three distinct mathematical objects. The device has a physical state, often described approximately by a density matrix $\rho$, a channel $\mathcal{E}$, or a Hamiltonian $H(\theta)$. The learner has a belief state, for example a posterior $p(\theta \mid D)$ or a neural surrogate $\hat{\mathcal{E}}_\phi$. The controller chooses an action $a$, such as a pulse, compilation route, fabrication parameter, or decoding decision. In an idealised decision-theoretic form, the next action maximises expected utility,

$$
a^* = \arg\max_a \; \mathbb{E}_{\theta \sim p(\theta \mid D)}[U(a, \theta)] ,
$$

where $U$ may encode gate fidelity, leakage suppression, decoding success, cost, latency, or robustness. The equation is simple; the engineering difficulty is that $U$ is only partially observed, the device drifts, and many measurements disturb the system being measured.

### AI-improves-hardware: calibration, control, and design

**Calibration** is the most immediate place where learning helps. Quantum processors contain many coupled parameters: qubit frequencies, crosstalk coefficients, drive amplitudes, mixer imbalances, readout thresholds, and time-dependent noise. Classical calibration can be viewed as system identification. Given observations $D = \{(a_i, y_i)\}$, a model learns a mapping from control actions to measured outcomes. The goal is not merely fitting a curve; it is choosing the next experiment that most efficiently reduces operational error. Bayesian optimisation, active learning, Gaussian processes, neural surrogates, and reinforcement learning can all play this role, but the best method depends on noise, dimensionality, safety constraints, and the cost of each experiment.

**Control** converts a target unitary $U_{\mathrm{target}}$ into hardware-native dynamics. On a real device the implemented operation is closer to

$$
\mathcal{E}_{u(t)}(\rho) = \operatorname{Tr}_E\left[V_{u(t)}(\rho \otimes \rho_E)V_{u(t)}^\dagger\right],
$$

where $u(t)$ is a pulse schedule and $E$ denotes uncontrolled environmental degrees of freedom. AI-assisted control searches over $u(t)$ while respecting amplitude limits, bandwidth, leakage penalties, and robustness to calibration drift. Learned policies can propose pulses; differentiable simulators can supply gradients; experimental feedback can correct simulation mismatch. These tools are not a licence to ignore physics: a pulse that looks optimal in a noiseless simulation may fail under shot noise, finite sampling, microwave distortion, or decoherence.

**Design** extends the same logic to slower cycles. Generative and surrogate models can explore qubit layouts, resonator geometries, photonic circuits, traps, materials candidates, or packaging choices. Here the learner often substitutes for an expensive simulator or fabrication run. The epistemic risk is larger: a model trained on a narrow design database may extrapolate confidently into regimes where manufacturing, materials stability, or hidden loss channels dominate. Responsible design loops therefore keep uncertainty estimates, human review, and experimental validation in the loop.

### Decoding and fault-tolerance thresholds

Fault tolerance introduces another feedback layer. An error-correcting code protects logical information by spreading it across physical qubits. A simplified structural statement of threshold behaviour is this: if the physical error rate $p$ lies below a code- and architecture-dependent threshold $p_{\mathrm{th}}$, increasing the code distance $d$ can suppress the logical error rate $p_L$; if $p$ is above threshold, larger codes may not help. Textbook scaling is often summarised schematically as

$$
p_L \approx A\left(\frac{p}{p_{\mathrm{th}}}\right)^{(d+1)/2},
$$

but the constants and the meaning of $p$ depend on correlated noise, leakage, measurement errors, connectivity, cycle time, and decoder latency. This formula should be treated as a structural guide, not a roadmap date.

Learned decoders attempt to infer likely error chains from syndrome measurements. The input is a stream of parity-check outcomes; the output is a correction or logical-frame update. AI can help when the noise is biased, correlated, nonstationary, or difficult to model analytically. The constraint is real-time operation: a decoder that is accurate but too slow cannot support a fault-tolerant computation. The important co-design question is not simply “which decoder is best?” but “which code, hardware layout, noise model, syndrome-extraction circuit, classical processor, and decoder together satisfy the latency and reliability requirements?”

### Hardware-may-improve-AI: conditional and open

The reverse direction must be phrased more cautiously. **Quantum data** are states, channels, measurements, or processes produced by quantum systems. For learning tasks whose data are inherently quantum, such as classifying phases from quantum states or learning properties of many-body dynamics, quantum processors may provide access that classical data pipelines cannot efficiently reproduce. This is the strongest conceptual route to quantum-native learning: the advantage, if established for a concrete task, comes from the data source and measurement model, not from a generic promise that quantum computers speed up all AI.

For classical machine learning, many proposed quantum speedups rely on assumptions about data loading, condition numbers, sparsity, oracle access, or output format. These assumptions can be valuable in a theorem, but they must be matched to an implementation. If preparing $|x\rangle$ from a classical vector $x$ costs as much as the alleged speedup saves, the end-to-end advantage may disappear. Dequantisation results further show that some quantum-inspired linear-algebra advantages can be matched by clever classical sampling. Thus the honest statement is open and conditional: future fault-tolerant quantum hardware may aid specific learning workloads under specific access models, but broad AI acceleration has not been established.

### Roadmaps as scenarios, not timelines

A roadmap is often read as a prediction. For a field with coupled scientific, engineering, economic, and supply-chain uncertainties, a better framing is **scenario analysis**. A scenario describes internally consistent conditions under which a class of systems becomes plausible. It does not assert that the conditions will occur by a named year.

- **Scenario A: calibration-scale co-design.** Devices remain noisy and modest in size, but AI-assisted calibration, pulse tuning, and layout-aware compilation improve experimental productivity. Claims are measured in reliability, uptime, and reproducibility, not universal advantage.
- **Scenario B: error-corrected specialisation.** Logical qubits become available for carefully selected workloads. AI contributes to decoders, scheduling, and resource estimation. Any advantage claim remains workload-specific and benchmarked against best classical methods.
- **Scenario C: quantum-data learning.** Quantum sensors, simulators, or processors generate data that are expensive to classically represent. Learning systems are co-designed around measurement constraints and physical symmetries. Advantage is a property of the full data-generation and inference pipeline.
- **Scenario D: stalled hardware, durable methods.** Hardware progress is slower than expected, yet AI tools for modelling, materials, control, and experimental design remain valuable to quantum science even without large-scale quantum computers.

These scenarios are not mutually exclusive. They emphasise that co-evolution can produce useful outcomes even if the most ambitious quantum-AI visions remain speculative.

### Co-design across the stack

The most interesting prospect is **co-design**: building hardware and algorithms together so that each is shaped to the other. A device whose native interactions match a learning model's structure, or a model whose ansatz respects a device's connectivity and noise, can outperform either component optimised in isolation. This mirrors the history of classical computing, where hardware and software co-evolved from general-purpose CPUs to specialised accelerators; the quantum analogue is only beginning. The trajectory, stated speculatively, points toward systems where the boundary between “the algorithm” and “the device” becomes less rigid.

Co-design can be analysed by layers:

- **Physical layer:** materials, fabrication, couplers, traps, modes, and loss channels.
- **Control layer:** pulses, gates, measurements, reset, scheduling, and feedback.
- **Compilation layer:** routing, native gate synthesis, noise-aware placement, and dynamical decoupling.
- **Learning layer:** ansätze, kernels, generative models, decoders, optimisers, and uncertainty estimates.
- **Evaluation layer:** baselines, ablations, resource accounting, and reproducibility.

A failure at any layer can erase gains elsewhere. Conversely, a modest improvement at several layers may matter more than a dramatic improvement in one isolated metric.

## From theory to code

The repository instantiates the AI-improves-hardware half of the loop across its modules — control, decoding, noise learning, materials design — each a runnable example of a method that feeds back into device development. Reading them together reveals the loop as a system rather than a list.

For these horizon chapters, code should be read as an audit scaffold rather than as evidence of quantum advantage. A minimal co-design record might track assumptions before any experiment is celebrated:

```python
from dataclasses import dataclass

@dataclass(frozen=True)
class CoDesignClaim:
    layer: str
    intervention: str
    baseline: str
    evidence: str
    open_risk: str

claim = CoDesignClaim(
    layer="control",
    intervention="learned pulse proposal with experimental recalibration",
    baseline="hand-tuned pulse schedule on the same device",
    evidence="to be measured under identical shot budget and drift conditions",
    open_risk="simulation-to-hardware mismatch and nonstationary noise",
)
```

The object is deliberately prosaic. It forces the author of a claim to name the layer, the intervention, the baseline, the evidence, and the unresolved risk. That discipline is often more valuable than a longer speculative program.

## Summary

AI and quantum hardware advance each other in a tightening loop, from learned calibration, control, decoding, and design to possible future uses of quantum data in learning. The AI-improves-hardware direction is already a practical engineering pattern, though every result still depends on validation under realistic noise and device constraints. The hardware-improves-AI direction is more open and should be stated conditionally, especially for classical data. Co-design is the horizon: not a guaranteed path to quantum advantage, but a systems methodology for aligning physical devices, control stacks, learning models, and honest evaluation.
