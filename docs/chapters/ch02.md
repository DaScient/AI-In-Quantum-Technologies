## Motivation

Why should a discipline founded on data have anything to say about quantum
physics, where first-principles equations are already known? The answer is
*tractability*. Schrödinger's equation is exact and useless at scale: the state
of $n$ qubits requires $2^n$ complex amplitudes. Machine learning offers
compressed, approximate representations that exploit the structure real physical
states actually possess — locality, symmetry, and low entanglement. AI does not
replace physics; it makes physics computable.

## Theory

We organise the AI toolbox by the question each paradigm answers.

**Supervised learning** maps inputs to labels by minimising a loss over examples.
In quantum science the labels are physical quantities — a molecular energy, a
critical temperature, the most likely error given a syndrome. The art lies in the
*representation*: a good descriptor respects the invariances of the problem
(permutation of identical atoms, rotation of a crystal) so the model need not
relearn them from data. This is the principle of **inductive bias**, and its
sharpest expression is the equivariant neural network, which builds symmetry into
the architecture itself.

**Unsupervised and self-supervised learning** discover structure without labels:
clustering phases of matter, compressing measurement records, or pre-training a
transformer on circuit corpora before fine-tuning on a downstream task. **Generative
models** — variational autoencoders, normalising flows, and diffusion models —
learn to *sample* from a distribution, enabling inverse design: ask for a crystal
with a target band gap and let the model propose candidates (Chapter 11).

**Reinforcement learning** treats discovery as sequential decision-making under
feedback. An agent that shapes a control pulse (Chapter 7) or schedules a
sequence of gates receives a reward — gate fidelity, circuit depth — and improves
by trial and interaction with a simulator or device.

Across all four paradigms runs a single pattern, which we will see again and
again: *an intractable quantum object is replaced by a parameterised function
trained to reproduce its measurable consequences.* The density matrix becomes a
neural network; the decoder becomes a classifier; the wavefunction becomes a
neural quantum state.

## From theory to code

The supervised pattern appears throughout `aiquantum`. The kernel-ridge
regressor for molecular energies (Chapter 10) is a minimal, transparent example:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.qchem import GroundStateRegressor

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
descriptors = rng.normal(size=(50, 8))
energies = descriptors @ rng.normal(size=8)      # a synthetic linear target
model = GroundStateRegressor(gamma=0.1).fit(descriptors, energies)
print(np.corrcoef(model.predict(descriptors), energies)[0, 1])
```

## Summary

Machine learning contributes not magic but *compression*: principled
approximations that respect physical structure. Choosing the right paradigm —
and encoding the right symmetries — is the recurring design decision of applied
quantum AI.
