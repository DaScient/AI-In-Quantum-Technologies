## Motivation

Why should a discipline founded on data have anything to say about quantum
physics, where first-principles equations are already known? The answer is
*tractability*. Schrödinger's equation is exact and useless at scale: the state
of $n$ qubits requires $2^n$ complex amplitudes. Machine learning offers
compressed, approximate representations that exploit the structure real physical
states actually possess — locality, symmetry, and low entanglement. AI does not
replace physics; it makes physics computable.

The most productive mindset is neither “let the neural network discover physics
from scratch” nor “derive everything analytically and ignore data.” Quantum
technologies sit between these extremes. Devices drift, materials have defects,
Hamiltonians are only approximately known, and measurements are expensive. AI can
learn surrogate models, discover patterns in experimental records, propose
candidate structures, and control systems under uncertainty. But the evidence
must be disciplined. A model that performs well on noiseless simulated data has
not thereby demonstrated hardware advantage, chemical accuracy, or robustness to
device noise. Simulation is a laboratory for hypotheses; hardware is a sampling
process with calibration, decoherence, and finite-shot constraints.

## Theory

We organise the AI toolbox by the question each paradigm answers.

### Supervised learning: predicting measured quantities

**Supervised learning** maps inputs $x$ to labels $y$ by minimising a loss over
examples. In quantum science the labels are physical quantities: a molecular
energy, a phase label, an error class given a syndrome, a qubit frequency shift,
or a control amplitude that produced high-fidelity behaviour in a prior
experiment. A typical objective is empirical risk minimisation,

$$
\hat{\theta}=\arg\min_\theta \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} L(f_\theta(x_i),y_i)+\lambda\Omega(\theta),
$$

where $L$ measures prediction error and $\Omega$ regularises complexity. The
model may be a kernel method, tree ensemble, Gaussian process, neural network, or
physics-informed architecture. The mathematical form matters less than the
alignment between representation and task.

Common supervised mappings include:

- **Regression**: descriptors of a molecule $\rightarrow$ ground-state energy,
  excitation energy, polarizability, or reaction barrier.
- **Classification**: syndrome bits $\rightarrow$ most likely error class;
  measurement snapshots $\rightarrow$ phase of matter.
- **Surrogate modelling**: circuit parameters $\rightarrow$ expected observable,
  avoiding repeated expensive simulation during outer-loop optimisation.
- **Calibration**: pulse settings and context $\rightarrow$ device response.

The art lies in the **representation**. A good descriptor respects invariances of
the problem — permutation of identical atoms, translation of a lattice, rotation
of a molecule, gauge conventions, or conservation laws — so the model need not
relearn them from data. This is the principle of inductive bias. A model with the
right bias may need fewer examples and extrapolate more safely; a model with the
wrong bias can fit the training set while violating basic physics.

### Validation discipline: train, validation, test

Machine-learning evidence must be separated into **training**, **validation**,
and **test** roles. Training data fit parameters. Validation data select
hyperparameters, architectures, descriptors, stopping points, and regularisation.
The test set is held back until the end to estimate generalisation. In quantum
work this split must respect physical structure. Randomly splitting near-duplicate
geometries, time-adjacent calibration records, or data from the same noise
realisation can leak information and inflate apparent performance.

A robust split asks what deployment means. If a model will predict unseen
molecules, entire chemical families may need to be held out. If it will decode an
error-correcting device, the test distribution should include realistic noise
drift rather than only the simulator distribution used for training. If it will
run in a laboratory feedback loop, finite-shot uncertainty must be represented in
validation. No single metric should be interpreted as proof of quantum advantage;
metrics are evidence about a specified task, distribution, and resource budget.

### Unsupervised and self-supervised learning: finding structure

**Unsupervised learning** discovers structure without labels. In quantum
technologies this often means clustering, dimensionality reduction, manifold
learning, anomaly detection, or density estimation on measurement records.
Examples include grouping phases of matter from snapshots, compressing tomography
data, identifying drift in calibration logs, or discovering latent coordinates
that organise materials simulations.

**Self-supervised learning** creates training signals from the data itself. A
model might mask parts of a measurement record and learn to reconstruct them, or
pre-train on circuit descriptions before being fine-tuned to predict depth,
noise sensitivity, or compilation choices. This is attractive because labelled
quantum data can be expensive, while unlabelled simulation outputs and device logs
may be plentiful. The risk is that a self-supervised objective can learn
shortcuts: file format conventions, simulator artefacts, or device-specific
signatures rather than transferable physics.

### Generative learning: sampling and inverse design

**Generative models** learn to sample from a distribution. Variational
autoencoders, normalising flows, autoregressive models, diffusion models, and
energy-based models can represent wavefunctions, propose molecules or materials,
or generate candidate circuits. In inverse design the direction of reasoning is
reversed: specify a desired property, such as stability or a target spectral
feature, and sample candidates likely to possess it.

Generative learning is powerful but easy to overstate. A generated molecule is a
hypothesis, not a discovery, until it survives physics-based screening,
uncertainty analysis, and experimental feasibility checks. A generated circuit is
not useful merely because it is syntactically valid; it must satisfy hardware
constraints, noise budgets, and task-level performance criteria. Generative
models are best treated as proposal engines coupled to verification pipelines.

A typical discovery loop is:

```text
physical priors + data
        │
        v
representation / descriptor ──> learning model ──> candidate prediction
        ^                              │                    │
        │                              v                    v
 validation evidence <──── simulator or hardware <── verification
```

The simulator branch provides controlled, reproducible feedback. The hardware
branch provides reality, but with sampling noise and operational constraints.
Both are valuable; neither should be confused with the other.

### Reinforcement learning: sequential decisions under feedback

**Reinforcement learning** treats discovery as sequential decision-making. An
agent observes a state $s_t$, takes an action $a_t$, receives a reward $r_t$, and
updates a policy $\pi(a|s)$. Quantum examples include pulse shaping, adaptive
measurement, circuit compilation, experiment scheduling, and error-correction
feedback. The reward may encode gate fidelity, circuit depth, energy reduction,
state-preparation success, or laboratory cost.

The central design challenge is reward specification. A reward that only favours
short circuits may choose gates that are fragile on hardware. A reward that only
favours simulated fidelity may exploit a model mismatch. A reward that ignores
measurement cost may be unusable in a laboratory. Good quantum RL is therefore
physics-aware RL: actions respect hardware constraints, simulators expose known
uncertainties, and learned policies are evaluated on held-out conditions before
being trusted.

### Symmetry, equivariance, and inductive bias

**Symmetry** is the most valuable source of inductive bias in quantum discovery.
If rotating a molecule should rotate vector features but leave scalar energy
unchanged, the model should encode that fact. If permuting identical atoms should
not change a prediction, the architecture or descriptor should be permutation
invariant. If a Hamiltonian conserves particle number, the variational ansatz or
feature representation should avoid wasting capacity on forbidden sectors.

Two terms are useful:

- **Invariant** outputs do not change under a transformation, such as molecular
  energy under global rotation.
- **Equivariant** outputs transform predictably, such as a force vector rotating
  with the molecule.

Equivariance is stronger and often more informative. It lets a model share
statistical strength across physically equivalent configurations and reduces the
burden on data. In quantum AI, symmetry enters kernel design, graph neural
networks, tensor networks, ansatz construction, and data augmentation.

### The recurring compression pattern

Across all paradigms runs a single pattern, which we will see again and again:
*an intractable quantum object is replaced by a parameterised function trained to
reproduce its measurable consequences.* The density matrix becomes a neural
network; the decoder becomes a classifier; the wavefunction becomes a neural
quantum state; the laboratory controller becomes a learned policy. The model is
not the system. It is a compressed interface to predictions, samples, decisions,
or explanations that can be validated against measurements and theory.

## From theory to code

The supervised pattern appears throughout `aiquantum`. The kernel-ridge
regressor for molecular energies (Chapter 10) is a minimal, transparent example:

```python
import numpy as np
from aiquantum.qchem import GroundStateRegressor

rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
descriptors = rng.normal(size=(50, 8))
energies = descriptors @ rng.normal(size=8)      # a synthetic linear target
model = GroundStateRegressor(gamma=0.1).fit(descriptors, energies)
print(np.corrcoef(model.predict(descriptors), energies)[0, 1])
```

This example is deliberately synthetic. It demonstrates the scikit-learn-style
pattern — `fit`, `predict`, inspect — without claiming any molecular benchmark.
In a real quantum-chemistry workflow, descriptors would encode molecular
geometry and composition, labels would come from trusted calculations or
measurements, and evaluation would use chemically meaningful held-out systems.

A more explicit train/validation/test sketch is:

```python
from sklearn.kernel_ridge import KernelRidge
from sklearn.metrics import mean_absolute_error
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split

x_train, x_temp, y_train, y_temp = train_test_split(descriptors, energies, test_size=0.4, random_state=0)
x_val, x_test, y_val, y_test = train_test_split(x_temp, y_temp, test_size=0.5, random_state=1)

candidates = [0.01, 0.1, 1.0]
best_model = None
best_val = float("inf")

for gamma in candidates:
    model = KernelRidge(kernel="rbf", gamma=gamma).fit(x_train, y_train)
    val_error = mean_absolute_error(y_val, model.predict(x_val))
    if val_error < best_val:
        best_val = val_error
        best_model = model

print(mean_absolute_error(y_test, best_model.predict(x_test)))
```

PennyLane supports the variational-classifier pattern. The following circuit is a
small simulator example, not a hardware performance claim:

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as pnp

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=2)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def classifier(features, weights):
    qml.AngleEmbedding(features, wires=[0, 1])
    qml.StronglyEntanglingLayers(weights, wires=[0, 1])
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliZ(0))

def prediction(features, weights):
    return pnp.sign(classifier(features, weights))

weights = pnp.zeros((2, 2, 3), requires_grad=True)
features = pnp.array([0.2, -0.4], requires_grad=False)
print(prediction(features, weights))
```

The same training logic as classical machine learning still applies: choose a
loss, optimise parameters on training data, tune on validation data, and report
only final generalisation on a held-out test set. If the circuit is moved from
`default.qubit` to hardware, finite shots and device noise become part of the
statistical model.

## Summary

Machine learning contributes not magic but *compression*: principled
approximations that respect physical structure. Supervised models predict
properties, unsupervised models reveal structure, generative models propose
candidates, and reinforcement learning policies make sequential decisions. The
recurring design decision of applied quantum AI is to choose the right paradigm,
encode the right symmetries, and validate under the distribution on which the
model will actually be used. Simulation is invaluable, but hardware evidence is
sampled, noisy, and operationally constrained.
