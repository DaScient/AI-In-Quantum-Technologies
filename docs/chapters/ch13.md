## Motivation

Cryptography is where the informational nature of quantum mechanics becomes a security guarantee. **Quantum key distribution** (QKD) lets two parties share a secret key whose secrecy rests not on unproven computational assumptions but on experimentally testable features of quantum theory: incompatible measurements, disturbance by observation, and the impossibility of cloning arbitrary unknown states. If an eavesdropper interacts with the quantum signals, the statistics observed by legitimate users change; secrecy becomes an engineering claim backed by an error budget rather than a promise that an adversary lacks enough computational power.

That physical guarantee does not make QKD a magic replacement for all cryptography. QKD distributes symmetric keys; it still needs an authenticated classical channel, careful detector engineering, and composable security proofs that account for finite data. At the same time, large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computers would undermine RSA, Diffie--Hellman, and elliptic-curve cryptography through Shor's algorithm. The practical security programme for the quantum era therefore has two tracks: deploy post-quantum public-key algorithms for ordinary digital infrastructure, and use QKD in settings where long-term confidentiality, specialised optical links, and operational discipline justify the hardware cost.

AI sits on both sides of this transition. It can stabilise QKD hardware, detect side-channel anomalies, optimise basis choices and decoy intensities, and accelerate finite-key inference. It can also sharpen traffic analysis, side-channel classification, and cryptanalytic search. Quantum security is consequently not a single protocol but a continuously monitored socio-technical system.

## Theory

### BB84 as a channel-estimation protocol

In the **BB84** protocol, Alice chooses a random bit $x \in \{0,1\}$ and a random basis $b \in \{Z,X\}$. In the computational basis she sends $|0\rangle$ or $|1\rangle$; in the diagonal basis she sends $|+\rangle=(|0\rangle+|1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$ or $|-\rangle=(|0\rangle-|1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}$. Bob independently chooses a basis $b'$ and measures. After many transmissions, Alice and Bob announce only their bases over an authenticated classical channel and retain the events with $b=b'$. This **sifting** step converts quantum measurements into a shared, but not yet secret, classical string.

The protocol works because the two bases are mutually unbiased. A measurement in the wrong basis produces no information about the encoded bit and disturbs the state relative to the original basis. An intercept-resend adversary, Eve, must guess a basis. When she guesses incorrectly, Bob receives a state that is random in Alice's basis, producing an error with probability $1/2$ on those rounds. Since Eve guesses incorrectly half the time, the simple attack contributes a $25\%$ error rate on tested sifted bits. Real channels also contain loss, dark counts, imperfect modulators, and detector noise, so the observed **quantum bit error rate** (QBER) is interpreted statistically rather than as a direct proof of an attacker.

A minimal pipeline is:

```text
Alice random bits+bases -> quantum states -> lossy/noisy channel -> Bob bases+measurements
         |                                                         |
         +------ authenticated public discussion: bases, tests -----+
                                      |
                          sifted key -> reconciliation -> privacy amplification
```

The public discussion leaks information, but it is controlled. A subset of bits is sacrificed for parameter estimation. If the QBER is acceptable, the remaining string is reconciled and compressed until Eve's possible information is negligible.

### Secret key rate and entropy terms

The Shor--Preskill security proof relates BB84 to entanglement purification and gives the asymptotic secret key rate per sifted bit as

$$
r = q\left[\,1 - H_2(e_b) - H_2(e_p)\,\right],
$$

where $H_2(e)=-e\log_2 e-(1-e)\log_2(1-e)$ is the binary entropy, $e_b$ is the bit error rate, $e_p$ is the phase error rate, and $q$ is the sifting efficiency. The engineering interpretation is direct:

- $H_2(e_b)$ is the asymptotic information disclosed during error correction, the cost of making Alice's and Bob's strings identical.
- $H_2(e_p)$ bounds Eve's information about the corrected string, the amount removed by privacy amplification.
- $q$ accounts for basis mismatch and protocol variants; standard BB84 has $q \approx 1/2$, while efficient biased-basis versions can approach $1$ in the key-generating basis.

For a symmetric channel with $e_b=e_p=e$, the bracket becomes $1-2H_2(e)$. It crosses zero at roughly $11\%$. Above that value, the asymptotic proof cannot certify positive secret key: the data are too noisy to distinguish a secure channel from one compatible with excessive adversarial information. This threshold is not a universal hardware limit; it depends on the protocol, proof technique, reconciliation efficiency, finite-key correction, detector assumptions, and whether two-way post-processing is allowed.

### From ideal proofs to deployed QKD

Security proofs use idealised Hilbert-space models, but deployed QKD is optical engineering. Weak coherent pulses may emit multiple photons, enabling photon-number-splitting attacks unless **decoy-state** methods estimate the single-photon contribution. Detectors have efficiency mismatch, dead time, afterpulsing, and saturation behaviour; adversaries exploit these as **side channels**. Measurement-device-independent QKD removes trust from the measurement device by letting Alice and Bob send states to an untrusted relay, trading hardware complexity for a cleaner security boundary.

Finite-key analysis is equally important. In a laboratory paper, asymptotic rates describe the limit of infinitely many rounds. In a field deployment, Alice and Bob observe a finite sample and must bound the probability that the unobserved rounds have worse errors. Security is expressed through a composable parameter $\varepsilon$: after the protocol, the produced key is within distance $\varepsilon$ of an ideal uniform secret key. This lets QKD keys be safely used inside larger cryptographic systems, provided authentication keys and failure probabilities are accounted for.

### AI in the quantum-security stack

**Machine learning** contributes most convincingly at the operational layer. Supervised classifiers can detect detector blinding signatures, timing shifts, polarisation drift, or abnormal afterpulse patterns. Bayesian models and neural estimators can improve channel-state tracking from sparse telemetry. Reinforcement learning can tune intensities, basis probabilities, and feedback loops for polarisation control under changing weather or fibre conditions. These tools should be treated as control and monitoring aids, not as replacements for security proofs: a neural classifier that misses an attack cannot define the security parameter.

AI also changes the threat model. Pattern-recognition systems improve power-analysis attacks, optical side-channel classification, and phishing or traffic-correlation campaigns against classical infrastructure surrounding a QKD link. For post-quantum cryptography, learning-based search and lattice reduction heuristics are part of the evaluation process. The mature posture is defence in depth: post-quantum algorithms for scalable authentication and signatures, QKD where physical key distribution is justified, and continuous anomaly monitoring around both.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.crypto` computes the BB84 rate, backing `/api/v1/crypto/key_rate`:

```python
from aiquantum.crypto import bb84_key_rate

print(bb84_key_rate(qber=0.02))   # positive -> secure
print(bb84_key_rate(qber=0.12))   # 0.0      -> above the threshold, insecure
```

The function is a compact offline reference for the asymptotic symmetric-channel calculation. It deliberately separates the theoretical rate from the many extra terms needed in a production finite-key analysis: reconciliation inefficiency, confidence intervals, authentication consumption, detector model, and decoy-state bounds.

A simple exploratory script can visualise how sharply entropy erodes the key rate:

```python
from aiquantum.crypto import bb84_key_rate

for qber in [0.00, 0.02, 0.05, 0.08, 0.11, 0.12]:
    rate = bb84_key_rate(qber=qber)
    status = "distil key" if rate > 0 else "abort"
    print(f"QBER={qber:0.2%}  rate={rate:0.4f}  decision={status}")
```

A Qiskit circuit can illustrate basis preparation and measurement, though it is a classical simulation of a tiny protocol fragment rather than a security proof or hardware QKD experiment:

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.primitives import Sampler

# Alice sends |+>; Bob measures in the X basis by undoing H before Z measurement.
qc = QuantumCircuit(1, 1)
qc.h(0)          # prepare |+>
qc.h(0)          # Bob's X-basis measurement rotation
qc.measure(0, 0)

result = Sampler().run(qc, shots=256).result()
print(result.quasi_dists[0])
```

The circuit shows basis compatibility, not QKD security. Real QKD uses calibrated optical states, authenticated public discussion, and statistical tests over long streams of detections.

## Summary

QKD turns physical law into a secrecy guarantee by converting disturbance into observable error statistics. BB84 uses incompatible bases, sifting, error correction, and privacy amplification; its asymptotic key rate is governed by binary entropy terms for bit and phase errors. Practical security requires finite-key proofs, authenticated classical communication, and careful treatment of optical side channels. AI is valuable for stabilisation, monitoring, and parameter optimisation, but it does not replace composable cryptographic proofs and can also enhance attacks.
