## Motivation

Fault-tolerant quantum computing rests on **quantum error correction** (QEC):
encoding a logical qubit across many physical qubits so that errors can be
detected and reversed without disturbing the stored information. The
computational bottleneck is *decoding* — inferring the most likely error from
syndrome measurements in real time — and it is here that machine learning, in the
guise of the textbook's `ErrorFormer`, has made dramatic gains.

## Theory

A stabiliser code is defined by a group of commuting Pauli operators, the
**stabilisers**, whose simultaneous $+1$ eigenspace is the code space.
Measuring the stabilisers yields a **syndrome** — a binary string that flags the
presence of errors without revealing the encoded state. The decoder's job is to
find the recovery operation most likely to have produced the observed syndrome.

The bit-flip **repetition code** is the simplest instructive example: a logical
bit is stored in $n$ physical qubits, and the parity checks $Z_i Z_{i+1}$ detect
disagreements. On a one-dimensional chain, the optimal decoder is
**minimum-weight perfect matching**: detection events are paired so as to flip the
fewest qubits, equivalent to a parity prefix-sum followed by a choice of the
lower-weight representative. For the two-dimensional **surface code**, matching
remains the workhorse, but its cost and its sub-optimality under correlated noise
motivate learned decoders. A neural decoder treats syndromes as inputs and
recoveries as labels (Chapter 5), and a transformer can exploit spatial and
temporal correlations that matching ignores, decoding faster and more accurately
in the regimes that matter.

**Error mitigation** is the near-term complement to correction. Rather than
encoding logical qubits, techniques such as zero-noise extrapolation and
probabilistic error cancellation post-process the outputs of noisy circuits to
estimate noise-free expectation values, trading additional sampling for accuracy.
Learned noise models (Chapter 6) sharpen these methods.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.quantum.error_correction` provides a minimum-weight repetition-code
decoder and a maximum-likelihood lookup decoder, backing
`/api/v1/quantum/error_decode`:

```python
from aiquantum.quantum import decode_repetition_code

recovery = decode_repetition_code([1, 1], n_data=3)  # middle-qubit flip
print(recovery, int(recovery.sum()))                  # weight-1 correction
```

## Summary

Error correction protects quantum information; decoding is its computational
heart. Classical matching is the baseline, and learned decoders extend it to the
correlated, real-time regime where fault tolerance will actually be won.
