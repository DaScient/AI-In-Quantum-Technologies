## Motivation

Quantum sensors are already the most mature quantum technology, powering atomic
clocks, gravimeters, and magnetometers. **Quantum metrology** asks how precisely a
parameter — a phase, a field — can be estimated given quantum resources, and how
entanglement can push precision beyond classical bounds. AI contributes on two
fronts: designing optimal probe states and extracting parameters from complex
measurement data.

## Theory

Suppose we estimate a phase $\phi$ imprinted on $N$ probes, repeated $\mu$ times.
With independent, unentangled probes, the uncertainty obeys the **standard quantum
limit** (SQL),

$$
\Delta\phi_\mathrm{SQL} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu N}},
$$

a manifestation of the central limit theorem. Entangled probes — GHZ or NOON
states — can reach the **Heisenberg limit**,

$$
\Delta\phi_\mathrm{HL} = \frac{1}{N\sqrt{\mu}},
$$

a quadratic improvement in $N$. The general bound is the **quantum Cramér–Rao
inequality**: for any estimator, $\Delta\phi \ge 1/\sqrt{\mu F_Q}$, where $F_Q$ is
the quantum Fisher information of the probe state with respect to the parameter.
Designing a sensing protocol is thus, in large part, the problem of maximising
$F_Q$ subject to the noise and resource constraints of a real device.

Machine learning enters where the idealisation breaks down. Under realistic
decoherence the optimal probe is neither GHZ nor classical but something in
between, and reinforcement learning can discover it. On the read-out side,
parameters are often buried in high-dimensional, correlated measurement records —
images from an atom array, time traces from a magnetometer — where neural
estimators outperform hand-crafted statistics. The textbook's `SensNet` learns to
place sensors optimally across a network, an instance of this design problem.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.metrology` exposes the three bounds, backing
`/api/v1/metrology/sensitivity`:

```python
from aiquantum.metrology import (
    standard_quantum_limit, heisenberg_limit, optimal_phase_sensitivity,
)

n = 16
print(standard_quantum_limit(n))           # 1/sqrt(N)
print(heisenberg_limit(n))                 # 1/N  -> quadratically better
print(optimal_phase_sensitivity(256.0))    # from a given quantum Fisher info
```

## Summary

Metrology quantifies how entanglement buys precision, from the standard quantum
limit to the Heisenberg limit, with the quantum Fisher information as the unifying
figure of merit. ML optimises probes under realistic noise and decodes complex
sensor data.
