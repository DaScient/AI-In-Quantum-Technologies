## Motivation

Quantum sensors are among the most mature quantum technologies, supporting atomic clocks, gravimeters, magnetometers, inertial sensors, and precision spectroscopy. **Quantum metrology** asks how precisely an unknown parameter — a phase, frequency, field strength, acceleration, or time offset — can be estimated given constrained physical resources. The central insight is that quantum states and measurements can reshape statistical uncertainty.

AI contributes in two complementary ways. First, learning algorithms can search for probe states, controls, and measurement settings that perform well under realistic noise rather than under ideal textbook assumptions. Second, neural estimators can extract parameters from high-dimensional data: fluorescence images, homodyne records, time traces, or distributed sensor readouts. These contributions are valuable only when benchmarked against statistical limits and classical baselines. A learned estimator cannot beat the quantum Cramér–Rao bound for the state and measurement resources it actually uses; it can, however, approach the relevant bound in regimes where hand-designed estimators are awkward.

## Theory

### Phase estimation model

A standard sensing model begins with a probe state $\rho_0$, an encoding channel that depends on an unknown parameter $\phi$, and a measurement. For unitary phase encoding,

$$
\rho_\phi = U_\phi \rho_0 U_\phi^\dagger, \qquad U_\phi = e^{-i\phi G},
$$

where $G$ is the generator. In an interferometer, $G$ may be a number-difference operator; for a spin ensemble, it may be a collective angular-momentum component. The experiment is repeated $\mu$ times, producing outcomes $x_1,\ldots,x_\mu$ drawn from

$$
p(x|\phi)=\operatorname{Tr}\!\left[M_x\rho_\phi\right],
$$

where $\{M_x\}$ is a POVM. An estimator $\hat{\phi}(x_1,\ldots,x_\mu)$ maps data to a parameter estimate.

This structure separates physics from statistics. The physics determines how $\phi$ is imprinted and what measurements are possible. The statistics determines how much information the observed data carry about $\phi$.

### Fisher information and Cramér–Rao bounds

For a fixed measurement, the **classical Fisher information** is

$$
F_C(\phi)=\sum_x p(x|\phi)\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\log p(x|\phi)\right)^2,
$$

with the sum replaced by an integral for continuous outcomes. Under standard regularity conditions, any unbiased estimator obeys the **Cramér–Rao bound**

$$
\operatorname{Var}(\hat{\phi}) \ge \frac{1}{\mu F_C(\phi)}.
$$

The **quantum Fisher information** (QFI) maximises this quantity over all measurements allowed by quantum mechanics:

$$
F_Q(\rho_\phi) = \max_{\{M_x\}} F_C(\phi).
$$

Thus

$$
\Delta\phi \ge \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu F_Q}}.
$$

For a pure state $|\psi_\phi\rangle=e^{-i\phi G}|\psi_0\rangle$, the QFI is especially simple:

$$
F_Q = 4\left(\langle G^2\rangle - \langle G\rangle^2\right)=4(\Delta G)^2.
$$

Large sensitivity therefore requires a probe state with large variance in the generator of the parameter. This formula also shows why entanglement can help: entangled states can have generator variance that scales quadratically with the number of probes.

### Standard quantum limit and Heisenberg limit

Suppose we estimate a phase $\phi$ imprinted on $N$ independent probes and repeat the experiment $\mu$ times. With separable probes, information adds linearly: $F_Q\propto N$. The uncertainty follows the **standard quantum limit** (SQL),

$$
\Delta\phi_\mathrm{SQL} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu N}},
$$

up to constants set by the generator and measurement convention. The $1/\sqrt{N}$ scaling is the quantum version of averaging independent samples; it reflects the central limit theorem.

Entangled probes can reach QFI proportional to $N^2$, giving the **Heisenberg limit**,

$$
\Delta\phi_\mathrm{HL} = \frac{1}{N\sqrt{\mu}}.
$$

The improvement is in scaling with $N$: $1/N$ rather than $1/\sqrt{N}$. This statement should be handled carefully. It assumes ideal state preparation, parameter encoding, and measurement. Under loss, dephasing, finite contrast, or imperfect readout, the asymptotic advantage may be reduced or removed. In practical sensing, the best probe is often not the most entangled ideal state but the state that maximises information after noise and resource costs are included.

### Probe states: coherent, squeezed, and GHZ

Product states are robust and easy to prepare. In a Ramsey experiment, $N$ atoms prepared independently in superposition acquire a phase, and measuring the collective spin gives SQL scaling.

**Squeezed states** redistribute quantum uncertainty between conjugate observables. A spin-squeezed state reduces noise in the measured quadrature at the cost of increased noise elsewhere. Such states can beat the SQL without requiring the fragility of a maximally entangled GHZ state.

A **GHZ state**,

$$
|\mathrm{GHZ}\rangle = \frac{|0\rangle^{\otimes N}+|1\rangle^{\otimes N}}{\sqrt{2}},
$$

accumulates phase coherently across all probes. Under $U_\phi=e^{-i\phi \sum_j Z_j/2}$, the relative phase evolves $N$ times faster than for one probe, yielding QFI proportional to $N^2$. The same collective sensitivity makes GHZ states fragile: a single lost or dephased particle can destroy the useful coherence.

### Adaptive measurements

In many sensors the best measurement depends on the unknown parameter. **Adaptive measurement** solves this by choosing later controls based on earlier outcomes. For example, an interferometer may update a feedback phase after each detection event to keep operation near the point of maximum slope. Bayesian phase estimation maintains a posterior distribution $p(\phi|\text{data})$ and chooses the next setting to reduce expected uncertainty.

The adaptive loop is naturally expressed as a decision process:

```text
prior p(phi)
    |
    v
choose probe state and control setting
    |
    v
parameter encoding on sensor
    |
    v
measure finite-shot data
    |
    v
update posterior or estimator
    |
    v
select next setting until budget is exhausted
```

Classical simulation can test policies across assumed noise models. Hardware execution adds finite calibration time, drift, detector imperfections, and the possibility that the simulated prior is wrong.

### AI for metrology and sensing

AI methods enter metrology at several points:

- **Probe-state design**: variational circuits, tensor-network ansätze, or reinforcement learning can search for states with high QFI under noise constraints.
- **Control design**: pulse sequences can be optimised to imprint the target parameter while suppressing nuisance parameters.
- **Adaptive measurement**: policies can choose feedback phases, interrogation times, or measurement bases.
- **Neural estimation**: models can map raw sensor records to parameter estimates, ideally with uncertainty calibration.
- **Sensor placement**: graph and geometric learning can select where to place sensors in a network subject to cost and coverage constraints.

These are optimisation and inference tasks, not evidence of unconditional quantum advantage. A learned protocol should be compared with analytic strategies, Bayesian estimators, Kalman filters, maximum-likelihood estimation, and classical experimental-design methods. The correct metric is not training loss alone but estimation error, calibration, robustness, and resource accounting against the relevant Fisher-information bound.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.metrology` exposes the three bounds, backing `/api/v1/metrology/sensitivity`:

```python
from aiquantum.metrology import (
    standard_quantum_limit, heisenberg_limit, optimal_phase_sensitivity,
)

n = 16
print(standard_quantum_limit(n))           # 1/sqrt(N)
print(heisenberg_limit(n))                 # 1/N  -> quadratically better
print(optimal_phase_sensitivity(256.0))    # from a given quantum Fisher info
```

The `aiquantum` functions are analytic reference calculations. They do not simulate detector noise, state-preparation error, or finite-shot estimator bias. Those effects must be included separately when modelling an experiment.

### PennyLane QFI sketch

The following PennyLane sketch computes the QFI matrix for a parameterised single-qubit sensing circuit. It is a simulator example: on hardware one would estimate probabilities or gradients from shots and include device noise.

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as np

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=1)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def sensing_state(theta):
    qml.RY(theta[0], wires=0)      # probe preparation
    qml.RZ(theta[1], wires=0)      # phase-like parameter encoding
    return qml.state()

theta = np.array([np.pi / 2, 0.1], requires_grad=True)
qfi_matrix = qml.metric_tensor(sensing_state)(theta)
print(qfi_matrix)
```

For pure states, this metric tensor is closely related to the QFI matrix, with convention-dependent factors. The entry associated with the encoded phase quantifies local distinguishability of nearby parameter values.

### Parameter-estimation snippet

A simple finite-shot estimation workflow samples a circuit at different candidate phases and chooses the maximum-likelihood value. This is intentionally classical post-processing of quantum measurement data.

```python
import numpy as np

shots = 200
observed_zeros = 146
phis = np.linspace(-np.pi, np.pi, 401)

# Ramsey-style model: P(0 | phi) = (1 + cos(phi)) / 2
p0 = (1.0 + np.cos(phis)) / 2.0
p0 = np.clip(p0, 1e-9, 1.0 - 1e-9)

log_likelihood = (
    observed_zeros * np.log(p0)
    + (shots - observed_zeros) * np.log(1.0 - p0)
)
phi_hat = phis[np.argmax(log_likelihood)]
print(phi_hat)
```

This estimator is only as good as its likelihood model. Real sensors may need nuisance parameters for contrast, dark counts, dephasing, and readout bias.

## Summary

Metrology quantifies how quantum resources improve parameter estimation. The SQL gives $1/\sqrt{N}$ scaling for independent probes, while the Heisenberg limit gives $1/N$ scaling under ideal entangled protocols. The quantum Fisher information and Cramér–Rao bound provide the unifying language for these limits. Squeezed and GHZ states show different trade-offs between enhanced sensitivity and fragility. AI improves practical sensing by optimising probes, controls, adaptive policies, and estimators under realistic constraints, but it must be evaluated against statistical bounds and hardware-aware baselines.
