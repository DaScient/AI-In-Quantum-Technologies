## Motivation

The promise of quantum computing rests on algorithms that provably outperform
their classical counterparts. Understanding *which* problems admit a quantum
speedup — and how large that speedup is — is the business of quantum complexity
theory. Increasingly, machine learning helps *discover* and *compile* the circuits
that realise these algorithms.

## Theory

A few algorithmic templates account for most known speedups. **Shor's algorithm**
factors integers in polynomial time by reducing factoring to period-finding,
solved with the quantum Fourier transform; it threatens classical public-key
cryptography (Chapter 13) and offers an exponential speedup. **Grover's
algorithm** searches an unstructured database of $N$ items in $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt
N)$ queries, a quadratic speedup that is provably optimal for the black-box model.
**Quantum simulation**, Feynman's original motivation, evolves a quantum system
forward in time exponentially faster than known classical methods for generic
Hamiltonians. **Quantum phase estimation** underlies many of these and connects to
the variational solvers of Chapter 17.

Complexity theory frames these results. The class **BQP** captures what quantum
computers solve efficiently; its precise relationship to **NP** is unknown, and it
is widely believed that quantum computers do *not* solve NP-complete problems in
polynomial time. **Sampling** problems — boson sampling, random-circuit sampling —
provided the first experimental claims of quantum advantage, being plausibly hard
for classical computers yet natural for quantum hardware. The boundary of
classical simulability is itself a moving front, repeatedly redrawn by better
tensor-network and learning-based simulators.

AI contributes to algorithm *engineering*. Reinforcement learning and generative
transformers — the textbook's `Q-GPT` — synthesise and compile circuits,
discovering shorter gate sequences and better qubit routings than human-designed
heuristics (Chapter 8's compilation theme). The peephole optimiser in this
repository is the transparent classical baseline for that learned compiler.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.quantum.circuit_optimize` reduces circuit depth by local rewrite rules,
backing `/api/v1/quantum/circuit_optimize`:

```python
from aiquantum.quantum import optimize_circuit

circuit = [{"gate": "H", "qubits": [0]}, {"gate": "H", "qubits": [0]},
           {"gate": "RZ", "qubits": [0], "param": 0.1},
           {"gate": "RZ", "qubits": [0], "param": 0.2}]
print(optimize_circuit(circuit))   # cancels H H, fuses the rotations
```

## Summary

Shor, Grover, simulation, and sampling map the terrain of quantum speedups, framed
by the complexity class BQP. AI increasingly discovers and compiles the circuits
that turn these algorithms into hardware-ready programs.
