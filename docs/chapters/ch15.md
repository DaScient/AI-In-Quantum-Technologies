## Motivation

The promise of quantum computing rests on algorithms that provably outperform their classical counterparts under clearly stated models. A quantum processor is not a universally faster computer; it is a device whose state space, interference, and measurement statistics make certain transformations cheaper. Understanding which problems admit a quantum speedup, and how large that speedup remains after input/output costs and error correction, is the business of **quantum complexity theory**.

The field has two complementary cultures. The first is mathematical: define a model, count queries or gates, and prove separations. Shor's factoring algorithm and Grover's search algorithm belong here. The second is engineering: map the algorithm to noisy hardware, reduce circuit_depth, choose error-correcting codes, and decide whether the total resource estimate beats a classical alternative. AI increasingly contributes to the engineering culture by discovering circuit identities, improving compilation, and searching ansatz families. It must not be allowed to blur the distinction between a promising circuit and a demonstrated advantage.

## Theory

### Computational models and complexity classes

A quantum computation applies a sequence of unitaries $U_1,U_2,\ldots,U_T$ to an initial state, often $|0\rangle^{\otimes n}$, and then measures. The final state is

$$
|\psi_T\rangle = U_T\cdots U_2U_1 |0\rangle^{\otimes n}.
$$

The probabilities are $p(x)=|\langle x|\psi_T\rangle|^2$. The power comes not from evaluating all answers in parallel and reading them out, but from arranging amplitudes so that wrong paths interfere destructively and useful structure survives measurement.

The class **BQP** contains decision problems solvable by a uniform family of polynomial-size quantum circuits with bounded error. It sits inside PSPACE and contains BPP, the classical bounded-error probabilistic class. Its relationship to **NP** is unknown. The standard belief is that quantum computers do not solve NP-complete problems in polynomial time, because known quantum speedups exploit algebraic structure, Fourier structure, simulation of quantum dynamics, or amplitude amplification rather than arbitrary combinatorial search.

Complexity claims require attention to the cost model. A query speedup counts oracle calls; a gate speedup counts elementary operations; an application speedup includes state preparation, data loading, error correction, measurement repetitions, and classical post-processing. Many proposed quantum machine-learning advantages weaken when the cost of loading classical data into amplitudes is included.

### Period finding and Shor's algorithm

**Shor's algorithm** factors integers by reducing factoring to order finding. Given an integer $N$ and a randomly chosen $a$ coprime to $N$, the order $r$ is the smallest positive integer with $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod N$. If $r$ is even and $a^{r/2}\not\equiv -1 \pmod N$, then

$$
\gcd(a^{r/2}-1,N) \quad \text{and} \quad \gcd(a^{r/2}+1,N)
$$

produce non-trivial factors with good probability. The quantum subroutine estimates the period of the function $f(x)=a^x \bmod N$ using the quantum Fourier transform (QFT). Modular exponentiation entangles an input register with periodic function values; measuring the second register leaves a periodic superposition in the first; the QFT converts periodicity into peaks from which continued fractions recover $r$.

```text
|0...0> --H-- modular exponentiation -- QFT^{-1} -- measure -- continued fractions
|1>     ------- controlled a^x mod N ----------- discarded/observed
```

The asymptotic speedup over the best known classical factoring algorithms is exponential. Its practical impact is cryptographic: RSA and finite-field or elliptic-curve Diffie--Hellman rely on problems that Shor's algorithm would solve efficiently on a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer. Present NISQ devices do not threaten deployed key sizes; the transition to post-quantum cryptography is driven by the long lifetime of sensitive data and the possibility of future decryption.

### Amplitude amplification and Grover search

**Grover's algorithm** addresses unstructured search. Given an oracle marking one solution among $N$ items, it finds a solution in $\mathcal{O}(\sqrt{N})$ oracle calls. The algorithm alternates two reflections: one flips the phase of marked states, and the other reflects about the uniform superposition. In the two-dimensional subspace spanned by marked and unmarked components, each Grover iteration rotates the state toward the solution by an angle proportional to $1/\sqrt{N}$.

The speedup is quadratic and optimal in the black-box model. That matters for security estimates: symmetric-key lengths can be doubled to compensate, but public-key systems based on factoring or discrete logarithms require replacement because Shor's algorithm changes the scaling class. Grover also illustrates an important theme: quantum algorithms frequently amplify a small useful amplitude rather than reveal all amplitudes.

### Quantum simulation, phase estimation, and variational methods

Feynman's original argument was that quantum systems are hard to simulate classically because their state space grows exponentially. A quantum computer can represent the state natively and implement Hamiltonian evolution

$$
U(t)=e^{-iHt}
$$

using product formulas, qubitisation, linear-combination-of-unitaries methods, or problem-specific decompositions. **Quantum phase estimation** (QPE) estimates eigenvalues of a unitary $U$ by applying controlled powers of $U$ and an inverse QFT. It is a core subroutine for chemistry, materials, and algorithms built on spectral information.

Fault-tolerant QPE can deliver high precision but requires deep coherent circuits. NISQ-era **variational quantum algorithms** trade depth for a hybrid loop: prepare $|\psi(\theta)\rangle$, measure an objective, update parameters classically, and repeat. This is not a guaranteed exponential speedup; it is a pragmatic architecture for limited hardware. Barren plateaus, shot noise, optimiser instability, and ansatz expressivity all affect performance.

```text
parameters theta -> quantum circuit U(theta) -> measurements -> classical loss
        ^                                                        |
        |---------------- optimiser update ----------------------|
```

### Sampling and quantum advantage claims

Sampling tasks ask a device to produce samples from a distribution believed hard for classical computers to reproduce. Boson sampling and random-circuit sampling are examples. They are attractive for experiments because they require less structure than full applications, but their interpretation is subtle. A claim of quantum advantage compares a specific quantum experiment with the best available classical simulation under a defined fidelity or cross-entropy criterion. It does not imply that useful business workloads have been accelerated, nor that all classical simulation is hopeless. Tensor-network contraction, stabiliser decompositions, approximate sampling, and high-performance computing repeatedly move the boundary.

A responsible statement is conditional: certain sampling experiments provide evidence that particular quantum devices perform specific distribution-sampling tasks beyond known classical methods at the time of analysis. The claim must be revisited as classical algorithms improve.

### AI for algorithm and circuit discovery

AI contributes to quantum algorithms through search over symbolic and continuous spaces. Reinforcement learning can discover gate sequences for small unitaries; graph neural networks can predict good qubit mappings; transformers can propose circuit rewrites; differentiable programming can optimise variational ansatz structure. These tools are best understood as accelerators for design and compilation rather than sources of unverified complexity claims.

The main optimisation targets are concrete:

- reduce two-qubit gate count, because entangling gates are usually noisier;
- reduce circuit_depth, because decoherence accumulates over time;
- respect hardware connectivity, avoiding excessive SWAP insertion;
- preserve observable estimates, not merely state-vector equality in an ideal simulator;
- minimise fault-tolerant resources such as $T$ count and magic-state demand.

A learned compiler must be validated against exact simulation for small cases, formal equivalence checking where possible, and hardware-aware benchmarks. Classical simulation of a proposed circuit is not the same as execution on real quantum hardware; simulation can ignore calibration drift, crosstalk, leakage, and shot noise unless those are explicitly modelled.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.quantum.circuit_optimize` reduces circuit depth by local rewrite rules, backing `/api/v1/quantum/circuit_optimize`:

```python
from aiquantum.quantum import optimize_circuit

circuit = [{"gate": "H", "qubits": [0]}, {"gate": "H", "qubits": [0]},
           {"gate": "RZ", "qubits": [0], "param": 0.1},
           {"gate": "RZ", "qubits": [0], "param": 0.2}]
print(optimize_circuit(circuit))   # cancels H H, fuses the rotations
```

This is a transparent peephole optimiser: adjacent self-inverse gates cancel, and compatible rotations fuse. Such local rules are not a complete compiler, but they expose the principle behind larger optimisation stacks. Circuit quality is judged by logical equivalence plus hardware-aware costs such as circuit_depth, two-qubit count, and error-corrected resource estimates.

Qiskit can show the same theme at circuit level. The following static example constructs a small redundant circuit and transpiles it for a basis gate set; it is illustrative and not a performance benchmark.

```python
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit, transpile

qc = QuantumCircuit(1)
qc.h(0)
qc.h(0)
qc.rz(0.1, 0)
qc.rz(0.2, 0)

optimised = transpile(qc, basis_gates=["rz", "sx", "x"], optimization_level=3)
print(qc.depth(), optimised.depth())
print(optimised)
```

PennyLane is natural for variational algorithms, where the quantum circuit is embedded in a classical optimisation loop:

```python
import pennylane as qml
from pennylane import numpy as np

dev = qml.device("default.qubit", wires=2, shots=None)

@qml.qnode(dev)
def energy(theta):
    qml.RY(theta, wires=0)
    qml.CNOT(wires=[0, 1])
    return qml.expval(qml.PauliZ(0) @ qml.PauliZ(1))

theta = np.array(0.2, requires_grad=True)
grad = qml.grad(energy)(theta)
print(float(energy(theta)), float(grad))
```

The PennyLane device is a classical state-vector simulator. Running an analogous circuit on hardware would require shot estimates, calibration awareness, queueing, and noise mitigation.

## Summary

Quantum algorithms obtain speedups by exploiting structure: periodicity in Shor's algorithm, amplitude amplification in Grover search, native Hamiltonian dynamics in simulation, and distributional hardness in sampling experiments. Complexity theory frames these results through BQP and related classes, while engineering determines whether an asymptotic advantage survives circuit depth, error correction, data movement, and noise. AI is increasingly useful for discovering ansatzes and compiling circuits, but every learned proposal must be checked against mathematical equivalence, resource estimates, and the distinction between classical simulation and real hardware execution.
