## Learning objectives

- Pose materials discovery as forward prediction and inverse design.
- Estimate superconducting critical temperatures from electron–phonon parameters.
- Understand generative models for crystal structures.

## Motivation

The materials that will host the next generation of qubits, sensors, and
superconductors are hidden in a combinatorial space far too vast to search by
hand. Machine learning accelerates discovery in two complementary directions:
**forward** prediction of a property from a structure, and **inverse** design of a
structure with a target property. The latter — generative materials design — is
among the most exciting frontiers of applied quantum AI.

## Theory

**Forward prediction.** Given a crystal, predict a property: band gap, formation
energy, or superconducting critical temperature $T_c$. For conventional
superconductors, the **McMillan** and **Allen–Dynes** formulas relate $T_c$ to
the electron–phonon coupling $\lambda$, the logarithmic-average phonon frequency
$\omega_\mathrm{log}$, and the Coulomb pseudopotential $\mu^*$:

$$
T_c = \frac{\omega_\mathrm{log}}{1.20}\,
\exp\!\left[\frac{-1.04(1+\lambda)}{\lambda - \mu^*(1 + 0.62\lambda)}\right].
$$

These physics-based estimators supply interpretable labels for training data and
serve as a sanity check on learned models. Data-driven regressors extend
prediction to unconventional superconductors where no closed form exists, learning
from databases such as SuperCon.

**Inverse design.** Here the task is to *generate* a crystal structure
conditioned on a desired property. Diffusion models — the family of the
textbook's `CrysDiff` — learn to denoise random lattices and atomic positions into
physically plausible structures, optionally guided toward a target. The output is
a unit cell: lattice vectors, species, and fractional coordinates, from which
density and symmetry follow. A reproducible baseline samples a structure and tunes
its cell volume to match a target mass density, establishing the data format and a
reference point for the learned generator.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.materials` provides both faces. The superconductor estimators back
`/api/v1/materials/superconductor`, and the crystal generator backs
`/crystal_generation`:

```python
from aiquantum.materials import allen_dynes_tc, generate_crystal

print(allen_dynes_tc(lambda_ep=0.8, omega_log=400.0, mu_star=0.1))  # T_c in K

structure = generate_crystal(["Cu"], target_density=8.96)
print(round(structure.density, 2), round(structure.volume, 2))      # ~8.96 g/cm^3
```

## Summary

Materials discovery couples forward prediction with inverse design. Physics-based
formulas give interpretable $T_c$ estimates; generative models invert the map,
proposing structures with desired properties.
