## Motivation

The materials that will host the next generation of qubits, sensors, and superconductors are hidden in a combinatorial space far too vast to search by hand. Each candidate is specified not only by a chemical formula but also by crystal symmetry, lattice constants, atomic sites, defects, strain, surfaces, and synthesis conditions. A small change in stoichiometry can turn a metal into an insulator; a defect that is harmful in one device can be the active quantum emitter in another.

Machine learning accelerates discovery in two complementary directions: **forward prediction** of a property from a structure, and **inverse design** of a structure with a target property. Forward models triage candidates before expensive density-functional theory (DFT), many-body simulation, or synthesis. Inverse models propose new unit cells or compositions that satisfy constraints. The strongest workflows do not treat AI as an oracle. They combine physics-based descriptors, uncertainty estimates, high-throughput computation, and experimental feedback.

Superconductivity illustrates both the promise and the risk. Conventional electron–phonon superconductors have interpretable theory that relates critical temperature to vibrational and coupling parameters. Unconventional superconductors, including cuprates and many correlated materials, do not reduce to a single closed-form estimator. AI can reveal patterns in databases, but it must not be allowed to hallucinate mechanisms or claim room-temperature superconductivity without reproducible evidence.

## Theory

### Periodic structures as data

A crystalline material is described by a **unit cell** with lattice vectors $\mathbf{a}_1,\mathbf{a}_2,\mathbf{a}_3$ and atoms at fractional coordinates $\mathbf{x}_i$. Translating by an integer combination of lattice vectors leaves the infinite crystal unchanged. A representation for learning must therefore respect:

- **Periodicity:** neighbours may lie across cell boundaries.
- **Space-group symmetry:** rotations, reflections, inversions, and glide operations can produce equivalent descriptions.
- **Permutation invariance:** swapping identical atoms cannot change a scalar property.
- **Composition and charge constraints:** oxidation states, electroneutrality, and site occupancy often restrict plausible candidates.
- **Stability:** predicted structures should be near a thermodynamic or kinetic basin, not merely syntactically valid.

Graph neural networks for crystals usually place atoms as nodes and periodic bonds as edges. Messages carry interatomic distances and sometimes angular information; pooling produces formation energy, band gap, elastic constants, or a superconducting proxy. The graph is not a molecule in a box. It is a finite representation of an infinite periodic object, and the neighbour list must include periodic images.

### Forward prediction and superconducting estimators

**Forward prediction** asks for a property $y$ given a material $x$: $y\approx f_\theta(x)$. The target might be formation energy, phonon stability, band gap, carrier mobility, dielectric response, magnetic ordering, or superconducting critical temperature $T_c$. Labels can come from experiments, high-throughput DFT, more expensive many-body methods, or physics formulas.

For conventional superconductors, the **McMillan** and **Allen–Dynes** formulas relate $T_c$ to the electron–phonon coupling $\lambda$, the logarithmic-average phonon frequency $\omega_\mathrm{log}$, and the Coulomb pseudopotential $\mu^*$:

$$
T_c = \frac{\omega_\mathrm{log}}{1.20}\,
\exp\!\left[\frac{-1.04(1+\lambda)}{\lambda - \mu^*(1 + 0.62\lambda)}\right].
$$

The formula is valuable because every parameter has physical meaning. Larger $\lambda$ tends to increase pairing, larger $\omega_\mathrm{log}$ raises the phonon energy scale, and $\mu^*$ suppresses pairing through screened Coulomb repulsion. It is not a universal law of superconductivity. It is an estimator for phonon-mediated pairing under assumptions that fail for many correlated materials.

Physics-based estimators serve three roles in AI pipelines:

- They provide interpretable labels or features when full calculations are unavailable.
- They define sanity checks; a model predicting high $T_c$ with tiny $\lambda$ should be questioned.
- They expose extrapolation risk because the denominator can become small or unphysical outside the formula's domain.

### Inverse design and generative crystals

**Inverse design** asks for $x$ given a desired property $y^*$. This is harder than forward prediction because the map from structure to property is many-to-one and constrained. A generated crystal must have valid geometry, reasonable density, plausible coordination, acceptable formation energy, and ideally a synthesis path.

Generative materials models include variational autoencoders, autoregressive composition models, diffusion models, and reinforcement-learning search. Diffusion models — the family of the textbook's `CrysDiff` — learn to denoise random lattices and atomic positions into physically plausible structures, optionally guided toward a target. At each denoising step, the model modifies fractional coordinates, lattice parameters, and species probabilities while preserving periodic structure.

A practical inverse-design loop looks like this:

```text
target property -> conditional generator -> candidate unit cells -> validity filters
       ^                                                        |
       |                                                        v
 experimental/DFT feedback <- uncertainty-aware ranking <- forward property model
```

The generator expands the search space; the forward model ranks; physics filters reject impossible candidates; DFT or experiments close the loop. Without the filters, a model may output crystals that match a learned data distribution but violate charge balance or collapse during relaxation.

### Materials for quantum technologies

Quantum technologies impose unusually severe materials requirements. Superconducting qubits need low microwave loss, stable tunnel barriers, clean interfaces, and controlled disorder. Spin qubits need isotopic purification and quiet magnetic environments. Photonic quantum devices need emitters with stable zero-phonon lines and low spectral diffusion. Quantum sensors exploit defects, but only defects with the right spin, optical, and coherence properties.

This shifts discovery from a single scalar target to a **multi-objective** problem. A candidate material may have an excellent band gap but unacceptable fabrication complexity; a superconductor may have high $T_c$ but excessive microwave loss. AI systems should therefore return Pareto fronts and uncertainty intervals rather than a single ranked list. Engineering decisions also require provenance: which data source, which relaxation settings, which synthesis assumptions, and which model version produced the prediction?

### Simulation, surrogate, and hardware boundaries

Most materials AI today is classical. DFT calculations, phonon solvers, and classical molecular dynamics generate data; ML models approximate or guide those tools. Quantum computers may eventually help with strongly correlated electrons, embedding problems, or real-time dynamics, but current devices are limited by qubit counts, noise, and measurement cost. A material screened by an ML model is not experimentally discovered; a material relaxed in DFT is not necessarily synthesised; and a toy Hamiltonian run on quantum hardware is not a validated industrial materials workflow.

## From theory to code

`aiquantum.materials` provides both faces. The superconductor estimators back `/api/v1/materials/superconductor`, and the crystal generator backs `/crystal_generation`:

```python
from aiquantum.materials import allen_dynes_tc, generate_crystal

print(allen_dynes_tc(lambda_ep=0.8, omega_log=400.0, mu_star=0.1))  # T_c in K

structure = generate_crystal(["Cu"], target_density=8.96)
print(round(structure.density, 2), round(structure.volume, 2))      # ~8.96 g/cm^3
```

The Allen–Dynes call is a deterministic physics calculation. The crystal-generation call establishes the unit-cell data structure used by learned generators and downstream filters.

A static screening sketch shows how the estimator can be wrapped in an uncertainty-aware workflow before any experimental claim is made.

```python
candidates = [
    {"name": "A", "lambda_ep": 0.7, "omega_log": 300.0, "mu_star": 0.10},
    {"name": "B", "lambda_ep": 1.1, "omega_log": 450.0, "mu_star": 0.13},
]

ranked = []
for row in candidates:
    tc = allen_dynes_tc(row["lambda_ep"], row["omega_log"], row["mu_star"])
    ranked.append((tc, row["name"]))

print(sorted(ranked, reverse=True))  # prioritise for deeper simulation, not for a press release
```

For quantum-circuit experimentation, a materials Hamiltonian can be reduced to a small spin or fermionic model and studied with an estimator primitive. Such a calculation is useful for algorithm development, but unless the Hamiltonian faithfully represents the material and the errors are controlled, it is not a materials prediction.

## Summary

Materials discovery couples forward prediction with inverse design. Physics-based formulas give interpretable $T_c$ estimates for conventional superconductors; graph and equivariant models extend prediction across periodic structures; generative models invert the map by proposing candidate crystals. The credible workflow is closed-loop and uncertainty-aware: generate, filter, simulate, measure, and update.
