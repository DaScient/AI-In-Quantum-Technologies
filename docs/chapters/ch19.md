# Chapter 19 — Ethical Dimensions of Quantum AI

> Part VII: Ethics, Policy, and Societal Impact

## Learning objectives

- Identify the ethical risks specific to the convergence of AI and quantum
  technology.
- Apply a structured auditing framework to a quantum AI system.
- Connect ethical principles to concrete engineering practices.

## Motivation

Powerful technologies carry responsibilities, and the convergence of two powerful
technologies compounds them. Quantum AI inherits the well-known hazards of machine
learning — bias, opacity, misuse — and adds quantum-specific ones, from the
cryptographic threat to the concentration of a scarce and expensive capability.
Treating ethics as integral to the engineering, not a postscript, is the stance of
this book.

## Theory

Several risk classes deserve explicit attention. **Security and privacy**: a
future quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could retroactively decrypt data
harvested today, the "harvest-now, decrypt-later" threat, making the post-quantum
migration an ethical imperative as much as a technical one (Chapter 13).
**Bias and fairness**: quantum-accelerated models trained on biased data
reproduce and can amplify discrimination, and the opacity of both deep networks
and quantum circuits makes such harms harder to detect. **Dual use**: tools for
materials discovery or optimisation can be turned to harmful ends, and the
designers bear some responsibility for foreseeable misuse. **Concentration of
power**: quantum hardware is capital-intensive and scarce, risking a widening gap
between those who possess the capability and those who do not (Chapter 21).
**Environmental cost**: training large models and operating dilution refrigerators
consume significant energy, a consideration in any honest accounting.

An ethical *audit* makes these abstractions actionable. A structured framework
examines a system across dimensions — data provenance and consent, model
transparency and contestability, security posture, fairness across affected
groups, environmental footprint, and governance and accountability — and produces
a documented, revisable assessment. Crucially, many of these checks map onto
engineering artefacts already advocated in earlier chapters: dataset
documentation and provenance (Chapter 5), validated and reproducible services
(Chapter 18), and transparent baselines against which learned components are
judged. Ethics, done well, is continuous with good engineering.

## From theory to code

The repository's fifth tutorial, *Ethical Audit of a Quantum AI System*, applies
this framework to an actual API model, walking through each dimension and
recording findings. The audit consumes the same provenance and reproducibility
artefacts the codebase already produces, demonstrating that ethical review can be
embedded in the development workflow rather than bolted on.

## Summary

Quantum AI inherits AI's ethical risks and adds quantum-specific ones —
retroactive decryption, capability concentration, dual use. A structured audit
turns principles into practice and aligns naturally with provenance,
reproducibility, and transparency.

## Exercises

1. List the audit dimensions and the engineering artefact that evidences each.
2. Explain the harvest-now-decrypt-later threat and its mitigation.
3. Draft three questions for a fairness review of a materials-screening model.
