## Motivation

Powerful technologies carry responsibilities, and the convergence of two powerful
technologies compounds them. Quantum AI inherits the well-known hazards of machine
learning — bias, opacity, misuse — and adds quantum-specific ones, from the
cryptographic threat to the concentration of a scarce and expensive capability.
Treating ethics as integral to the engineering, not a postscript, is the stance of
this book.

The practical reason is simple: once a model is embedded in a pipeline, its
choices become institutional choices. A hybrid classifier used to prioritise
molecular candidates may influence which diseases receive research attention. A
quantum-enhanced optimiser may decide which infrastructure plans are explored and
which are silently discarded. A machine-learning service that helps discover new
materials can contribute to batteries and catalysts, but adjacent capabilities may
also be relevant to surveillance, weapons, or strategic resource extraction. The
ethical question is therefore not whether a circuit state $|\psi\rangle$ is
"moral". It is whether the socio-technical system built around preparation,
measurement, data selection, optimisation, deployment, and governance distributes
benefit and harm responsibly.

The same discipline used for robust engineering applies here. A system whose
training data cannot be traced, whose benchmarks cannot be reproduced, and whose
failure modes are undocumented is not merely inconvenient; it is ethically weak.
Conversely, a system with clear assumptions, calibrated uncertainty, security
review, and accountable ownership is easier to challenge and improve. Ethical
practice is not a substitute for technical excellence. It is one of its tests.

## Theory

### A socio-technical view of quantum AI

**Quantum AI** should be evaluated as a full pipeline rather than as an isolated
algorithm. The quantum component may be a variational circuit, a kernel estimator,
a simulator, a chemistry subroutine, or a source of samples. The AI component may
perform representation learning, surrogate modelling, active learning, or decision
support. Ethical responsibility attaches to the interface among all of these
parts: what data are admitted, what objective is optimised, what uncertainty is
reported, who can contest outputs, and what downstream use is anticipated.

A useful blueprint is:

```text
Data source -> consent/provenance -> feature map -> quantum/simulated evaluation
            -> classical optimiser -> model card -> deployment monitor
            -> incident response and responsible disclosure
```

The diagram deliberately includes governance artefacts in the same line as
technical artefacts. In a hybrid model, a density matrix $\rho$ may be transformed
by a unitary $U(\theta)$ and measured to produce features for a classical learner.
The ethical analysis asks additional questions: whose data are encoded into
$\rho$ or into the parameters that prepared it; whether $U(\theta)$ is deployed on
real hardware or in classical simulation; how measurement noise, shot noise, and
hardware drift are represented; and whether the final decision is used for
screening, ranking, exclusion, or discovery. A harmless notebook demonstration can
become consequential when connected to a procurement, health, security, or credit
decision.

Several risk classes deserve explicit attention. **Security and privacy**: a
future quantum computer running Shor's algorithm could retroactively decrypt data
harvested today, the "harvest-now, decrypt-later" threat, making the post-quantum
migration an ethical imperative as much as a technical one (Chapter 13).
**Bias and fairness**: quantum-accelerated models trained on biased data
reproduce and can amplify discrimination, and the opacity of both deep networks
and quantum circuits makes such harms harder to detect. **Dual use**: tools for
materials discovery or optimisation can be turned to harmful ends, and the
designers bear some responsibility for foreseeable misuse. **Concentration of
power**: quantum hardware is capital-intensive and scarce, risking a widening gap
between those who possess the capability and those who do not (Chapter 21).
**Environmental cost**: training large models and operating dilution refrigerators
consume significant energy, a consideration in any honest accounting.

### Dual-use risk and foreseeable misuse

**Dual use** means that a capability designed for beneficial purposes can also be
used for harmful ones. In quantum AI this is not limited to cryptography. Search,
optimisation, simulation, and generative design can support medicine, logistics,
climate modelling, and materials discovery; the same abstract capabilities may
assist chemical design, evasive optimisation, or strategic surveillance. Ethical
analysis should avoid sensational claims about imminent quantum advantage where it
has not been demonstrated. The more grounded point is that research trajectories
can lower barriers, automate expertise, or concentrate capabilities in ways that
change the threat landscape.

A practical dual-use review asks:

- What concrete capability does the system improve: speed, scale, accuracy,
  automation, or access?
- Which harmful applications become easier if the tool is released without
  controls?
- Can risk be reduced through staged release, rate limits, monitoring,
  documentation, or restricted access to sensitive models or datasets?
- Are there legitimate users who would be unfairly excluded by a control, and how
  can appeals or alternative access be provided?

The goal is neither secrecy by default nor openness without reflection. It is
context-sensitive stewardship. A tutorial showing a toy variational classifier is
normally low risk. A high-throughput closed-loop system optimising hazardous
molecular properties demands a different release decision.

### Fairness and bias propagation through hybrid models

**Fairness** concerns the distribution of model errors, benefits, and burdens
across affected groups. Quantum acceleration does not cleanse a dataset. If a
training set under-represents a population, a quantum kernel or variational
feature map can preserve that imbalance in a transformed Hilbert space. If labels
reflect historical discrimination, optimisation may faithfully reproduce the
pattern. Even in scientific domains that seem far from human classification,
selection bias matters: a materials dataset may over-represent compounds that
well-funded laboratories chose to study, leading an active learner to exploit the
known region while neglecting alternatives.

The mathematical form is familiar. A model estimates $\hat{y}=f_\theta(x)$, where
$\theta$ may include quantum circuit parameters and classical weights. A fairness
review compares error rates, calibration, or utility across groups $g$:

$$
\Delta_{\mathrm{err}} = \max_g |\Pr(\hat{y}\neq y\mid g) - \Pr(\hat{y}\neq y)|.
$$

This expression is not a universal fairness definition; it is an example of an
auditable disparity. Different contexts require different metrics. Equalised odds,
calibration, demographic parity, subgroup robustness, and distribution-shift
testing can conflict with one another. Ethical engineering therefore requires a
justified choice of metric, not the mechanical reporting of a single number.

Quantum-specific complications include finite-shot estimates, device noise, and
simulator-versus-hardware gaps. A fairness result obtained with a noiseless
classical simulator may not hold on a noisy processor. If group membership
correlates with feature regions that are more sensitive to noise or encoding
angle, hardware variation can create unequal error. Claims should state whether
results come from exact simulation, shot-based simulation, or real quantum
hardware, and should include uncertainty intervals rather than a single polished
score.

### Transparency and explainability of hybrid models

**Transparency** is the ability to understand how a system was built and how it
should be used. **Explainability** is the ability to provide meaningful accounts
of specific outputs. Hybrid quantum-classical models challenge both. Classical
neural networks are already opaque; parameterised unitaries add transformations in
high-dimensional complex vector spaces. Measurement further compresses the state:
we do not inspect $|\psi\rangle$ directly, but estimate observables from samples.

Yet opacity is not an excuse. Several forms of explanation remain available:

- **Model transparency**: publish the circuit ansatz, feature map, optimiser,
  loss function, data provenance, hardware or simulator settings, shot counts, and
  random seeds where appropriate.
- **Behavioural explanation**: compare against simple baselines, ablate features,
  test counterfactual inputs, and report where the hybrid model fails.
- **Uncertainty explanation**: separate epistemic uncertainty from finite-shot
  sampling noise and hardware calibration drift.
- **Decision explanation**: document whether the model is advisory, autonomous,
  or one input into human review.

A useful engineering analogy is the lab notebook. A quantum AI system need not be
fully interpretable at the level of every amplitude, but it must be inspectable at
the level needed for replication, challenge, and safe use. Documentation cannot
prove virtue, but the absence of documentation prevents accountability.

### Environmental cost and responsible scaling

**Environmental cost** includes energy used to train large AI models, run
classical simulations of quantum systems, operate cryogenic hardware, and repeat
experiments under noisy conditions. It would be misleading to quote universal
energy figures without context: the footprint depends on hardware, cooling,
utilisation, region, workload, and accounting boundary. The ethical requirement is
therefore transparent accounting rather than invented precision.

Responsible scaling asks whether the marginal scientific value justifies the
marginal resource use. Before running a large experiment, teams should establish
classical baselines, small-scale simulations, stopping rules, and reuse plans for
data. If a quantum model is run on real hardware, the report should clarify why a
simulator or classical baseline was insufficient. If the experiment is exploratory,
that status should be explicit. Environmental review is not anti-science; it is a
way to spend scarce experimental and computational resources on questions worth
answering.

### Responsible disclosure of cryptographic threats

**Responsible disclosure** is the coordinated process of reporting vulnerabilities
or threat-relevant findings to affected parties before public release. Quantum
computing creates a distinctive disclosure problem because cryptographic risk may
be time-shifted. Data encrypted today can be stored and attacked later if a
cryptographically relevant quantum computer becomes available. The ethical duty is
therefore not to wait for a dramatic breakthrough announcement. Organisations
should inventory cryptographic assets, prioritise long-lived secrets, migrate to
post-quantum schemes as standards and implementations mature, and communicate risk
without exaggeration.

Disclosure should be factual and proportional. A paper improving a toy attack on
small RSA instances is not evidence that deployed public-key infrastructure has
collapsed. Conversely, dismissing harvest-now-decrypt-later risk because a large
fault-tolerant machine does not yet exist ignores the long lifetime of medical,
legal, diplomatic, and identity data. The responsible path is early migration
planning, clear threat models, and coordination among researchers, vendors,
standards bodies, and affected users.

### Audit as engineering practice

An ethical *audit* makes these abstractions actionable. A structured framework
examines a system across dimensions — data provenance and consent, model
transparency and contestability, security posture, fairness across affected
groups, environmental footprint, and governance and accountability — and produces
a documented, revisable assessment. Crucially, many of these checks map onto
engineering artefacts already advocated in earlier chapters: dataset
documentation and provenance (Chapter 5), validated and reproducible services
(Chapter 18), and transparent baselines against which learned components are
judged. Ethics, done well, is continuous with good engineering.

An audit should be periodic rather than one-off. New data, hardware calibration,
policy changes, and downstream uses can invalidate an earlier judgement. The most
important audit output is often not a pass/fail label, but a risk register with
owners, mitigations, open questions, and review dates.

## From theory to code

The repository's fifth tutorial, *Ethical Audit of a Quantum AI System*, applies
this framework to an actual API model, walking through each dimension and
recording findings. The audit consumes the same provenance and reproducibility
artefacts the codebase already produces, demonstrating that ethical review can be
embedded in the development workflow rather than bolted on.

A small local checklist can make the review concrete. The following snippet is
illustrative: it records auditable fields and computes a simple group error gap.
It is not a universal fairness test and does not require quantum-framework code.

```python
from dataclasses import dataclass
from typing import Iterable

@dataclass
class AuditFinding:
    dimension: str
    evidence: str
    risk: str
    owner: str


def group_error_gap(y_true: Iterable[int], y_pred: Iterable[int], groups: Iterable[str]) -> float:
    rows = list(zip(y_true, y_pred, groups))
    overall = sum(yt != yp for yt, yp, _ in rows) / len(rows)
    gaps = []
    for group in sorted({g for _, _, g in rows}):
        subset = [(yt, yp) for yt, yp, g in rows if g == group]
        err = sum(yt != yp for yt, yp in subset) / len(subset)
        gaps.append(abs(err - overall))
    return max(gaps, default=0.0)


findings = [
    AuditFinding("provenance", "dataset card v2", "medium", "data steward"),
    AuditFinding("transparency", "ansatz and seeds documented", "low", "model lead"),
    AuditFinding("security", "PQC migration plan pending", "high", "security lead"),
]
```

In a real audit the checklist would be stored with model metadata, review notes,
and mitigation status. The key discipline is traceability: every ethical claim
should point to evidence that another reviewer can inspect.

## Summary

Quantum AI inherits AI's ethical risks and adds quantum-specific ones —
retroactive decryption, capability concentration, dual use. A structured audit
turns principles into practice and aligns naturally with provenance,
reproducibility, and transparency. Fairness analysis must account for biased data,
finite-shot uncertainty, simulator-versus-hardware differences, and downstream
use. Responsible disclosure and environmental accounting convert abstract concern
into operational duties.
